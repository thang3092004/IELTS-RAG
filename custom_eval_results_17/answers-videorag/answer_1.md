The definition of what constitutes a 'powerful' company has undergone significant transformation from the 20th to the 21st century, driven largely by technological advancements and the changing dynamics of global markets. 

### Historical Context

In the 20th century, power in the corporate world was primarily associated with size and scale. Companies that boasted a large workforce, substantial market share, and significant physical assets were regarded as powerful. For instance, multi-national corporations like General Electric and ExxonMobil exemplified this model, thriving due to their ability to leverage vast resources and a robust operational scale. Power was often equated with the ability to withstand market fluctuations and maintain dominance through established processes and systems.

### Shifts in the 21st Century

As we moved into the 21st century, the criteria for corporate power began to shift dramatically. Several factors contributed to this change:

1. **Agility vs. Size**: Agility emerged as a key asset, overshadowing sheer scale. Companies that could swiftly adapt to market changes and innovate rapidly gained competitive advantage. For instance, Tesla, founded in the early 2000s, became a leader in the automotive industry despite being young compared to established giants. This highlights that responsiveness and the capability to pivot quickly can outweigh traditional notions of size.

2. **Technological Integration**: The rise of digital technologies transformed business operations. Companies that embraced digital transformation and cloud services found new ways to scale and reach customers far beyond traditional methods. Microsoft, for example, shifted its focus from software sales to cloud services, illustrating a significant transformation in its business model to remain relevant.

3. **Market Capitalization and Tech Giants**: The early 21st century has seen the ascent of technology companies, with a focus on market capitalization becoming a key metric of power. In 2017, for example, companies like Apple, Amazon, and Alphabet (Google) emerged at the forefront, often surpassing traditional powerhouses in market value. The emphasis moved from tangible assets to intangible ones, such as innovation, brand loyalty, and customer engagement.

4. **Sustainability and Corporate Responsibility**: Modern consumers increasingly value corporate responsibility and sustainability. Thus, companies demonstrating commitment to social and environmental issues are regarded as powerful within today's contexts. Corporate missions, like Microsoft’s goal to "empower every person and organization on the planet to achieve more," reflect a broader societal focus that resonates with stakeholders beyond shareholders.

### Conclusion

In summary, the definition of a 'powerful' company has evolved from mere size and resource accumulation to a more nuanced understanding that encompasses agility, technological integration, market adaptability, and corporate responsibility. The 21st century champions flexibility and innovation, where companies must leverage their capabilities to maintain relevance and compete effectively in a rapidly changing global landscape. As we continue further into this century, the bar for corporate power may continue to shift, driven by emerging technologies and changing consumer expectations.
