The limited number of examples needed for reinforcement fine-tuning is significant because reinforcement learning algorithms can learn from a much smaller dataset than traditional fine-tuning. Reinforcement learning models are trained by observing the model's outputs and receiving rewards or penalties based on those outputs. The key insight is that the model can learn from a much smaller number of examples than traditional fine-tuning, which is very useful for real-world applications that have limited data. For example, in medical diagnosis, doctors can only diagnose a few patients with a new disease. The reinforcement fine-tuning model can learn from a small number of patient records to diagnose new diseases, which is not feasible with traditional fine-tuning.
