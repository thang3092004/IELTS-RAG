The sources describe a protein identification task where, given a patient’s symptoms, the goal is to predict which gene is responsible for the genetic disease. Justin Ree, a researcher at Berkeley Lab, worked with OpenAI to train different models on this task, using a dataset of case reports on rare genetic diseases. Here’s a comparison of how the three models performed:

* **o1 Mini:**  This was the baseline model for the reinforcement fine-tuning process. On the evaluations dashboard, o1 Mini achieved a **top-at-one accuracy of 17.7%**, meaning it correctly identified the responsible gene as the first item in the ranked list 17.7% of the time. It had top-at-five accuracy of 42% and top-at-max accuracy of 57.1%. 
* **o1:** This model, released just before reinforcement fine-tuning was introduced, showed an improvement over o1 Mini.  Its top-at-one accuracy was **25%**. 
* **Reinforcement Fine-tuned o1 Mini:** This model demonstrated the effectiveness of reinforcement learning on this specific task. By training o1 Mini with reinforcement learning using a grader that evaluated the model’s ranked list of genes, its performance significantly improved, surpassing even o1. The fine-tuned o1 Mini achieved a **top-at-one accuracy of 31%**. 

**The results show that reinforcement fine-tuning o1 Mini significantly enhanced its ability to accurately identify the responsible gene for a given set of symptoms, even outperforming the larger o1 model on this specific task.**  It highlights the potential of reinforcement learning for customizing large language models to excel in specialized domains.
