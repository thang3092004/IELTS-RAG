In the sources, a distinction is made between brokerages operating on an "A book" and a "B book." The source material explains that all brokerages operate on a combination of the two. 

The important distinction between the two is how the brokerage handles a client's orders.  

*   **"B book" brokerages essentially take the opposite side of a trade from a client when they think the client is likely to lose.** For example, if the client buys a currency, the brokerage sells the same currency, hoping the client will lose and they will profit. This method is sometimes referred to as **"taking the other side of the trade"**.
*   **"A book" brokerages will place a client's order into the market, allowing them to interact directly with other traders.** This is done when the brokerage believes the client is a more skilled trader.

Brokerages tend to use both methods, depending on their assessment of a client's skill level. They want clients to trade as they make money on the spread (the difference between the bid and ask price), but if they believe a client is likely to lose money, they will "take the other side of the trade" to profit. 

The source material notes an important consideration for traders: **"All of this kind of stuff goes on inside brokerages, which is another reason why I like to have personal communication with ... with the broker"**.
