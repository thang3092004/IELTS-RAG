The United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS) has employed the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) alongside Decision Lens software as part of its “Continuous Distribution” model, significantly improving the efficiency of organ allocation and consequently reducing deaths on organ transplant waiting lists. 

### Overview of UNOS and Its Challenges

UNOS serves as the primary organization for coordinating all donations and transplants of organs in the United States. Historically, they faced challenges with their organ allocation system, which often resulted in a backlog of candidates waiting for organs. Prior to the adoption of AHP and Decision Lens, the allocation process was not sufficiently effective, leaving a significant number of candidates waiting for transplants and, tragically, succumbing while on the waiting list.

### Implementation of AHP and Decision Lens

The integration of AHP and Decision Lens allowed UNOS to make more data-driven and structured decisions regarding organ distribution. AHP, a decision-making framework that helps prioritize various criteria, was utilized to establish the importance of multiple factors influencing organ allocation. These factors included medical urgency, compatibility (such as blood type), candidate age, and logistical considerations like travel efficiency.

Decision Lens software provided UNOS with an interactive platform to analyze these factors systematically. The software helps identify optimal allocation strategies by computing various scenarios based on inputs regarding urgency and potential outcomes. This approach not only helps streamline the decision-making process but also ensures that more weight is placed on critical criteria that impact successful transplant outcomes.

### Results Achieved

The outcomes emphasized in the presentation highlighted a remarkable reduction in waitlist deaths following the implementation of these tools. Under the previous system, where 438 candidates were reported to have died waiting for transplants, the introduction of continuous allocation strategies—fueled by AHP and Decision Lens—lowered the number to 208 deaths. This significant drop indicates that the new model is not only enhancing operational efficiency but is also prioritizing candidates in a manner that maximizes survival likelihoods.

Furthermore, the analysis of post-transplant survival probabilities and the improvement of organ placement efficiency helped reinforce the decision frameworks within UNOS. The emphasis on survival rates over simple wait times transformed how the organization evaluates success in organ transplant logistics.

### Conclusion

By leveraging AHP and Decision Lens, UNOS has made substantial progress in reducing mortality on transplant waiting lists. These tools created a more responsive and equitable allocation framework, enabling healthcare professionals to effectively prioritize candidates based on multiple, nuanced factors. This evidence-based approach is a critical step towards reducing not only wait times but also the number of lives lost while patients wait for life-saving organs.
