The National Park Service (NPS) employs a structured approach for prioritizing environmental cleanup projects through its Environmental Compliance and Cleanup Division (ECCD). This system is crucial for addressing contamination issues within national parks and ensuring environmental sustainability. Below are the key criteria that highlight how the NPS prioritizes its cleanup efforts. 

### Hierarchical Model of Criteria

The ECCD utilizes a hierarchical model that breaks down overall objectives into specific goals, criteria, and subcriteria. This structured approach helps in systematically evaluating and ranking different projects based on their urgency and potential impact. The main components include:

1. **Goals**: The overarching goals of the ECCD focus on several essential areas such as:
   - Protecting human health.
   - Enhancing access to public lands.
   - Reducing contamination and its backlog.
   - Stimulating the economy through better environmental health.

2. **Criteria**: For each of the goals, the NPS identifies specific criteria against which cleanup projects are evaluated. These criteria assess:
   - **Public Benefit**: How well a project serves the public interest and enhances park visitor experiences.
   - **Environmental Protection**: The extent to which the project will mitigate environmental harm and promote ecological restoration.
   - **Effectiveness in Reducing Contamination**: Evaluating how effectively the project can eliminate or reduce contaminants at the site.

3. **Subcriteria**: The subcriteria further break down the criteria into specific measurable aspects, making the decision-making process more nuanced and comprehensive. Examples include:
   - Pollution prevention measures.
   - Improvements in water and air quality.
   - The community's involvement and collaborative efforts in cleanup initiatives.

### Decision-Making Tools

To facilitate the assessment process, the NPS utilizes decision-making frameworks like **Decision Lens** and the **Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP)**. These tools allow for:
- Weighing the importance of various factors as they apply to different projects.
- Ensuring that funds and resources are allocated efficiently across multiple contaminated sites without favoritism.
- Analyzing trade-offs between environmental benefits and investment costs, which is essential for executing meaningful cleanup efforts.

### Historical Context and Legacy Issues

Another critical aspect of their criteria involves addressing historical land use practices that may have led to significant contamination issues, such as unremediated silver mines. By incorporating legacy pollution concerns, the NPS aims to tackle some of the more severe environmental challenges present within national parks effectively.

### Conclusion

The National Park Service's approach to prioritizing environmental cleanup projects is comprehensive and multi-faceted. By employing a hierarchical model of criteria, effective decision-making tools, and addressing historical environmental concerns, the NPS ensures that its cleanup initiatives align with broader public health and ecological goals. This systematic prioritization not only enhances the effectiveness of cleanup projects but also supports the preservation of national park resources for future generations.
