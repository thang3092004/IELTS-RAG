Organ allocation decisions are complex and involve many factors beyond just medical urgency. The allocation of organs to patients is guided by criteria that consider a wide range of patient characteristics, including age, medical history, and social factors. These criteria aim to maximize the chances of a successful transplant and ensure that patients receive the best possible outcome after the transplant. However, the allocation of organs is not always perfect, and there are still cases of 'futile transplants' where organs are allocated to patients who are unlikely to survive. Reducing the number of futile transplants is important to increase the overall success rate of organ transplantation.

To address this issue, the United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS) developed an algorithm called decision lens. This algorithm uses the decision lens model to allocate organs to patients based on a variety of criteria, including medical urgency, medical history, and social factors. The algorithm is able to consider a wide range of factors and make trade-offs across different organs to optimize the allocation of organs.

By using the decision lens algorithm, UNOS was able to reduce the overall wait list death rate by 45%. The algorithm also helped to avoid futile transplants by prioritizing highly sensitized candidates who were unlikely to survive. This has resulted in an increase in the number of successful organ transplants and improved the overall survival rate for patients who receive organs.

In summary, the importance of considering post-transplant outcomes like patient survival rates and avoiding futile transplants cannot be underestimated in organ allocation decisions. Algorithms like decision lens are powerful tools that can help to optimize allocation decisions and maximize the chances of a successful transplant outcome.
