The transition from episodic to continuous planning has significantly changed how organizations utilize tools like Decision Lens and the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP). Previously, planning was often conducted episodically, for example, on an annual or semi-annual basis. This approach involved using the AHP to establish a static model of value and priorities, which would then be used to evaluate a portfolio of options. This traditional method is akin to taking a snapshot of the current environment and providing a fixed answer. 

However, the increasing digitization of planning tools, like Decision Lens, has led to a shift towards continuous planning. This means that planning is now an ongoing process, constantly adapting to changing circumstances.  The sources highlight the following key aspects of this transition:

* **Dynamic Decision-Making:** In a continuously changing environment, organizations need to be able to constantly re-evaluate their priorities and make adjustments to their plans. Continuous planning enables organizations to respond more effectively to unexpected events, like the situation in Ukraine, which had a significant impact on the US Navy's priorities for ships, airplanes, bases, and personnel.
* **Integration and Connectivity:** Continuous planning necessitates integration across different time horizons, functions, and hierarchical levels within an organization.  Decision Lens facilitates this integration by connecting plans across time, ensuring that short-term actions align with long-term objectives. It also enables planning across different functions, breaking down silos and fostering a more holistic approach, as exemplified by the US Navy's need to integrate planning for airplanes, ships, power, and base operations. Furthermore, Decision Lens connects planning up and down the organizational hierarchy, allowing for alignment between high-level strategic objectives and the day-to-day operations at lower levels.
* **Real-Time Adaptation and Scenario Planning:** With Decision Lens, organizations can make changes to their plans on the fly, adding or removing factors as needed, rather than relying on a static model. This allows for real-time adaptation to evolving circumstances. The software's capability to run millions of permutations quickly on different resource scenarios also enables organizations to conduct extensive scenario planning, exploring various options and identifying the best path forward.

In essence, the transition from episodic to continuous planning has transformed tools like Decision Lens and the AHP from static, snapshot-based models into dynamic, movie-like systems that allow organizations to constantly adapt and optimize their plans in response to a rapidly changing world.
