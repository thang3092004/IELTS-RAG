Decision Lens software, which incorporates the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP), has significantly enhanced the organ allocation decisions of the United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS) by improving equity and effectiveness.  Here's how:

**Reduced Mortality:** Decision Lens enabled UNOS to develop a comprehensive model for organ allocation that considers a wider range of relevant criteria. This resulted in a substantial decrease in mortality rates for patients waiting for organ transplants. For example, deaths related to lung transplants declined by over 50%.

**Key Criteria Considered:**  The software facilitated the incorporation of critical factors into the decision-making process, moving beyond the simplistic consideration of travel time. These factors included:

*   **Medical urgency:** The software considers the urgency of the transplant based on the recipient's medical condition. 
*   **Post-transplant outcomes:**  The model takes into account the likelihood of the transplant's success and the candidate's post-transplant survival rate.  This helps avoid futile transplants, where the recipient has a low chance of survival or benefit.
*   **Equity:** Decision Lens helps UNOS address disparities in transplant access by considering factors such as a candidate's socio-economic background, blood type, and prior living donor status.  This ensures that organs are allocated based on need and not just on logistical convenience. 
*   **Efficiency:**  While not the primary driver, the system still considers the efficiency of organ placement, including travel time, to optimize the allocation process.

**Impact:** By using Decision Lens, UNOS has shifted from a reactive, localized approach to a more proactive, data-driven system. This has led to a more effective and equitable allocation of organs, reducing waitlist deaths and improving post-transplant survival rates.  The success of this approach has led to the adoption of AHP in the entire US transplant distribution system.
