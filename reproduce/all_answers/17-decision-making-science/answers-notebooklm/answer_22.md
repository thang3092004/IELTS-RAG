When allocating organs for transplantation, it's crucial to consider not just medical urgency but also post-transplant outcomes. **The goal is to maximize the benefit of the donated organ by ensuring it goes to a recipient who has the highest chance of survival and a good quality of life after the transplant.**

One key factor is the candidate's **post-transplant survival rate**, which is considered during the evaluation process. This helps **avoid "futile transplants," where an organ is given to someone with a very low likelihood of survival even with the transplant.** For instance, a lifelong alcoholic receiving a liver transplant might have a low chance of survival if they haven't addressed their alcohol addiction.

By considering post-transplant outcomes, organ allocation organizations can:

* **Increase overall transplant success rates.**
* **Reduce the number of deaths** on the waiting list. 
* **Ensure that organs are used effectively and ethically**, giving recipients the best possible chance at a longer, healthier life. 

The United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS), responsible for organ allocation in the United States, has successfully implemented this approach, leading to significant declines in death rates for those awaiting lung transplants. They are now applying similar criteria to kidneys, pancreases, and other organs.
