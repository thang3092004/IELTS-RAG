The United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS) partnered with Decision Lens and employed the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) to develop a more effective method for allocating organs. Prior to implementing this system, UNOS primarily focused on minimizing travel time between donor and recipient, which proved to be an inefficient allocation strategy. By utilizing the AHP and Decision Lens software, UNOS created a comprehensive model for evaluating potential recipients and identifying the most suitable matches for available organs. This approach enabled UNOS to significantly decrease the number of deaths on organ transplant waiting lists, reducing fatalities by over 50% for lung transplants. UNOS is now expanding the application of this model to other organ types, including kidneys and pancreases.

Here's a detailed look at how UNOS leveraged the AHP and Decision Lens software:

*   **Developed a multi-faceted criteria**: The model incorporated a range of factors beyond travel time, allowing for a more holistic assessment of each potential recipient.

    *   **Medical Urgency**: The model evaluated the medical urgency of the transplant, considering factors like pediatric priority levels. This ensured that patients in most critical need were given priority.
    *   **Post-transplant Outcomes**: The model included criteria to evaluate candidates' post-transplant survival, aligning with regulations to avoid futile transplants. This ensured that organs were allocated to recipients with the highest likelihood of successful outcomes.
    *   **Equity**: The model incorporated a criterion for reducing biological disadvantages in transplant access, prioritizing highly sensitized candidates based on their CPRA (calculated panel reactive antibody) score. This aimed to address disparities and provide equitable access to organ transplants for all patients.
    *   **Other Factors**: The model also considered candidate size, prior living donor status, age group, and travel time.
*   **Software facilitated complex analysis**: Decision Lens software enabled UNOS to run millions of permutations on different resource scenarios based on the AHP model. This allowed for a level of analysis that would be impossible for humans to perform manually, ensuring that each organ was allocated to the most suitable recipient based on the defined criteria.

The combination of the AHP's structured decision-making framework and the computational power of Decision Lens software allowed UNOS to move beyond a simplistic approach and develop a sophisticated, data-driven system that prioritizes both patient need and transplant success, ultimately saving lives.
