The Defense Health Agency (DHA) uses a set of criteria to balance the well-being of individual warfighters with the overall mission effectiveness of the Department of Defense (DoD) when making resource allocation decisions. The DHA uses the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) and decision lens software to evaluate different investment alternatives.

The DHA's process considers the following criteria:

*   **Impact on the operational effectiveness of the warfighter**:  This criterion prioritizes investments that will maintain or improve the warfighter's ability to perform their duties. 
*   **Impact on the medical readiness of the warfighter**: This criterion considers the ability of an investment to minimize the time a warfighter is unavailable for duty due to injury or illness. 
*   **Ability to enable short-term recovery and restore health**: This criterion focuses on investments that support the warfighter's immediate recovery following an injury or illness.
*   **Ability to enable long-term rehabilitation**: This criterion considers investments that contribute to the warfighter's long-term health and well-being.
*   **Current criticality of the capability gap and requirement**: This criterion examines the urgency of the need being addressed by a particular investment.
*   **Impact on the quality of life of the greater population**: This criterion considers the broader societal impact of a warfighter’s injury or illness, including resource needs from the civilian population. 

These criteria demonstrate that the DHA prioritizes mission effectiveness in its resource allocation decisions, placing greater weight on factors that contribute to the warfighter's ability to carry out the DoD's mission. However, the DHA also acknowledges the importance of the warfighter's individual well-being, including their short-term and long-term recovery. Notably, the criteria consider the well-being of the broader population, recognizing the potential impact of a warfighter’s injury on society at large.
