Quantifying the value of security investments is a major challenge for many organizations. Traditionally, security spending has been viewed as an expense rather than an investment. This is because it is difficult to demonstrate a tangible return on investment (ROI) for security measures, especially those that aim to prevent incidents from happening in the first place.

One way that organizations can attempt to justify security investments is by conducting studies that compare organizations with robust security architectures to those with less robust security. Such studies can reveal that organizations that have invested in stronger security measures experience fewer security breaches, faster identification of adversaries within their systems, and reduced ransom payments. Microsoft commissions studies like this to provide customers with the data they need to support their arguments for increased security spending.

Another approach is to shift the focus from preventing all attacks to **disrupting the economics of attacks**. For financially motivated attackers, like ransomware operators, making it more expensive or time-consuming to attack an organization can be an effective deterrent. Organizations can do this by implementing measures that make it harder for attackers to gain initial access to systems.

For example, **multi-factor authentication (MFA)** is a relatively inexpensive security measure that can significantly increase the cost and effort for attackers attempting to use stolen credentials.  **Password spraying**, a technique where attackers use lists of commonly used passwords to attempt to gain access to accounts, is a low-effort, low-cost attack method that can be very successful. By implementing MFA, organizations can force attackers to move on to more complex, costly, and time-consuming attack methods.

While disrupting the economics of attacks can be effective against financially motivated attackers, this approach may be less effective against attackers who are motivated by ideology or are nation-state actors. For these types of attackers, organizations need to focus on protecting sensitive information and implementing layers of controls to make it more difficult for attackers to achieve their objectives.
