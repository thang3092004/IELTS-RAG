When using smaller LLMs, there are several challenges that can arise. First, the models are less powerful and less capable than larger models, so they are better suited for simpler tasks. For example, smaller models are not as good at coding and reasoning, so they may struggle with complex coding tasks. 

Second, smaller models have fewer parameters, so they have less capacity to learn and generalize from data. This means they may not be able to learn as much from limited data, so their performance may be limited.

Finally, smaller models are less efficient and have lower VRAM usage, so they may not be able to handle as many requests or as large of a model size. This can impact their performance and scalability.

In summary, smaller LLMs have less power, capacity and efficiency compared to larger models, so they are better suited for simpler tasks but may struggle with more complex or data-limited tasks.
