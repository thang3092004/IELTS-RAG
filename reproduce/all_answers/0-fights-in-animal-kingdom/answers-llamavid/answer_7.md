Freshly molted crabs have a limited amount of energy to grow into their new shells. To ensure they have enough time to expand and harden their new shells before the next high tide, they must decide when to molt. Their timing depends on several factors:

1. The tide cycle - They need to wait until the tide is low enough for them to escape the shallows and move into deeper water before molting.

2. The weather - They must molt when the weather is favorable, as they are vulnerable to predators on the beach.

3. The crab's own biological clock - They need to molt when their body is ready for molting, which is triggered by hormones and environmental cues.

4. The crab's social structure - The crabs know when other crabs in their group have molted, which helps determine the optimal time.

In summary, freshly molted crabs use a combination of environmental, biological, and social factors to determine the optimal time for their mass molting event. They must balance the need to grow into a new shell with the need to avoid predators and other threats.
