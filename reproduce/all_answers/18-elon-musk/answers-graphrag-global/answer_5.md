## Observation of Neuronal Activity Levels

The activity levels of most neurons are primarily characterized by low firing rates, indicating that many neurons tend to remain stably inactive most of the time. This phenomenon implies that a typical neuron is not continuously firing, as inactivity is common within neural circuits. The activity can be sporadic and varies based on both internal and external stimuli, suggesting that while neurons may show low levels of engagement at baseline, they have the capacity to become intensely active under the right conditions. This condition is often referred to as the Neural Non-stationarity Effect, highlighting that neuronal engagement may change significantly.

## Comparison to Astrophysical Concepts

Analysts draw several compelling parallels between the behavior of neurons and phenomena observed in astrophysics. For example, the activity levels of neurons can be likened to dark matter in the universe, which constitutes a substantial portion of cosmic matter that is not directly observable but influences gravitational dynamics. This analogy emphasizes the critical, albeit often hidden, role that inactive or low-activity neurons play in the overall function and dynamics of the brain.

Furthermore, the neuronal behavior exhibits patterns reminiscent of the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) fluctuations, where apparent random activity reveals significant trends at multiple levels of complexity. This suggests that just as astrophysical researchers strive to understand complex cosmic structures, neuroscientists seek to unravel how stable and often low neuronal activity contributes to broader brain functions.

Additionally, the fluctuation in neuronal activity can be compared to celestial phenomena such as the dormant states of stars that may align or synchronize their activity to create noticeable cosmic events. This emphasizes the idea that while most neurons remain quiet, they possess the potential to become active in response to specific stimuli, similar to how dormant celestial bodies may react under certain conditions.

In conclusion, these observational insights into neuronal activity levels not only reveal the dynamic nature of how neurons interact and engage but also establish a fascinating intersection of neuroscience and astrophysics, suggesting that complex systems across different fields share underlying principles.
