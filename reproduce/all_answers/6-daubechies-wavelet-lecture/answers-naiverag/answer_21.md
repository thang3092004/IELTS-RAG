In the discussion surrounding the cepstrum for extracting signal components, Daubechies acknowledges several critical limitations that impact its effectiveness. These limitations primarily stem from the inherent nature of the techniques and the types of signals being analyzed. 

### Non-Sinusoidal Waveforms
One significant limitation mentioned is the challenge posed by non-sinusoidal waveforms. Daubechies notes that while the cepstrum is a useful tool for signal analysis, it often falls short when dealing with signals that do not conform to sinusoidal patterns. This irregularity can lead to complications in accurately extracting meaningful amplitude and frequency information from the signals.

### Stability Under Noise
Another concern raised by Daubechies revolves around the stability of the cepstrum when signals are subjected to noise. She points out that the presence of noise can significantly alter the results obtained through cepstral analysis, making it difficult to deduce precise characteristics of the signal. As noise can introduce parasitic fluctuations within the signal, ensuring the robustness of the cepstrum in noisy environments becomes a challenging task.

### Empirical Mode Decomposition 
Additionally, Daubechies references her work on empirical mode decomposition and its associated splines, highlighting the complexities introduced by oscillations and non-constant functions. The extraction process can lead to issues such as parasitic maxima, which form when subtracting non-constant terms from a signal, complicating the analysis further.

### Conclusion
In summary, while the cepstrum provides valuable insights into signal components, Daubechies emphasizes the need to address challenges related to non-sinusoidal signals, noise interference, and the complexities arising from empirical mode decomposition. These factors necessitate caution and thorough consideration when utilizing the cepstrum in various applications of signal processing.
