The mathematical structures that underpin time-frequency representations are rooted in concepts from **Fourier analysis, group theory, and the analysis of operators on Hilbert spaces.** These representations aim to analyze signals that vary in both time and frequency, going beyond the standard Fourier transform that only provides frequency information without time localization.

Here are some key mathematical structures involved:

*   **Windowed Fourier Transform (WFT):**  This is a fundamental tool for time-frequency analysis. It involves multiplying a signal by a window function centered at different time points and then computing the Fourier transform of the windowed signal. This allows for localized frequency information at different times.
*   **Gabor Frames:** These are families of functions constructed by translating and modulating a window function in the time-frequency plane. They form a redundant representation of a signal, meaning there are multiple ways to represent the signal using the frame elements. Gabor frames provide flexibility in time-frequency analysis, but they are not orthogonal bases, meaning their elements are not mutually orthogonal.
*   **Tight Frames:** These are special types of frames that provide a reconstruction formula similar to orthonormal bases, simplifying the process of recovering the original signal from its frame coefficients.
*   **Heisenberg Group:** This is a non-commutative group that describes translations and modulations in the time-frequency plane. The non-commutativity captures the inherent uncertainty principle in time-frequency analysis, where precise localization in both time and frequency is impossible.
*   **Zak Transform:** This is a unitary transform that maps functions from the time domain to a space of periodic functions on the unit square. It's a valuable tool for analyzing the properties of functions related to time-frequency representations, particularly in proving the Balian-Low theorem.
*   **Balian-Low Theorem:**  This theorem states that it's impossible to construct an orthonormal basis of functions that are well-localized in both time and frequency simultaneously. This highlights a fundamental limitation in time-frequency analysis and motivates the use of redundant representations like frames.
*   **Resolution of Identity:** This is a concept used to represent the identity operator as a sum of projection operators onto different subspaces. In time-frequency analysis, this idea is used to decompose the identity operator into a sum of rank-one projection operators corresponding to different time-frequency regions, effectively providing a way to analyze a signal by localizing it in the time-frequency plane.
*   **Localization Operators:** These are operators that, when applied to a signal, focus on a specific region in the time-frequency plane. Analyzing their properties, such as their eigenvalues and eigenfunctions, can reveal insights into the time-frequency characteristics of the signal.
*   **Prolate Spheroidal Wave Functions:** These are eigenfunctions of a specific differential operator that arises in the analysis of band-limited signals observed over finite time intervals. They have excellent localization properties in both time and frequency, making them useful in signal processing tasks involving band-limited signals.
*   **Synchro-Squeezing:** This is a technique that improves the resolution of time-frequency representations by "squeezing" the energy of a signal in the time-frequency plane along frequency trajectories, resulting in sharper and more accurate representations.

These mathematical structures provide a framework for understanding and analyzing signals that vary in both time and frequency. They are utilized in various applications, including signal processing, image analysis, music analysis, and medical diagnostics.
