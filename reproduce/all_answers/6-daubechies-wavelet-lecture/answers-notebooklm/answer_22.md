Daubechies suggests that **biologists are becoming increasingly interested in analyzing animal vocalizations**, particularly birdsongs, using time-frequency methods. She highlights the need to **move beyond spectrograms**, the current primary tool for birdsong analysis, to more sophisticated techniques that can extract meaningful parameters from the complex signals. 

Daubechies identifies several potential research directions in this area:

* **Developing methods to extract shape functions from birdsong recordings.** This would allow researchers to go beyond simply identifying the presence of harmonics and instead understand the underlying structure of the sounds.
* **Creating a transform that is adapted to the specific shape functions of the birdsongs being studied.** This could involve using dual functions and leveraging the knowledge gained from the extracted shape functions.
* **Improving the robustness of time-frequency methods when dealing with limited data.** This is crucial as many animal vocalizations, unlike human speech or electrocardiograms, may not contain numerous periods for analysis.

These advancements would enable biologists to:

* **Generate synthetic birdsongs that closely mimic natural ones.** This would be invaluable for conducting controlled experiments to investigate how birds learn and the role of regional dialects in their vocalizations.
* **Gain deeper insights into the neurological processes underlying birdsong learning and production.** 

Daubechies emphasizes the potential for **interdisciplinary collaborations** between biologists, signal processing experts, and mathematicians to drive progress in this field.
