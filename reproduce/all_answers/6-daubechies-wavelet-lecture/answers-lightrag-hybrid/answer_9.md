The "no-go theorem" concerning orthonormal bases in the context of time-frequency analysis relates to the limitations and challenges of constructing such bases. This theorem is particularly significant in the study of signal processing and mathematical signal representations. 

### Understanding Orthonormal Bases

An orthonormal basis consists of functions that are not only orthogonal but also normalized. This means that each function in the basis is distinct (orthogonal) from the others, and the length of each function is one (normalized). In time-frequency analysis, these bases are crucial for accurately representing signals with both time and frequency information.

### The No-Go Theorem

The essence of the "no-go theorem" is that, under certain conditions, it is fundamentally impossible to create a complete orthonormal basis that can simultaneously localize signal properties in both time and frequency domains. This theorem highlights several key points:

1. **Limited Degree of Freedom**: The theorem suggests that in the realm of finite-dimensional spaces, there are theoretical limitations on how well one can encode information in both time and frequency using orthonormal bases. 

2. **Trade-offs Between Time and Frequency Localization**: While it is possible to have a basis that is well-localized in one domain (either time or frequency), achieving good localization in both simultaneously is inherently constrained. This is often illustrated through the use of uncertainty principles, which state that increasing localization in time will lead to increased spread in frequency and vice versa.

3. **Implications for Signal Processing**: The limitations described by the "no-go theorem" profoundly affect practical applications like signal processing. It demonstrates that while one can strive for certain resolution in time or frequency, achieving both to an ideal extent is not feasible.

### Conclusion

The no-go theorem serves as a critical reminder of the theoretical limits present when constructing orthonormal time-frequency bases. It informs researchers and practitioners about the inherent trade-offs and constraints they must navigate in the analysis and representation of signals across these domains. Understanding these limitations is essential for developing realistic models and methods in time-frequency analysis. 

In essence, this theorem underscores the balance researchers must strike between time and frequency localization, fundamentally shaping approaches in both theoretical and applied mathematics.
