In the context of Ingrid Daubechies' work and presentations, she acknowledges several limitations inherent in the use of cepstrum methods for extracting signal components. Here are some critical points regarding these limitations:

### Complexity of Non-Sinusoidal Waveforms

One significant limitation that Daubechies points out is the challenge posed by non-sinusoidal waveforms. Traditional cepstrum analysis, which is designed primarily for sinusoidal signals, may not perform adequately when dealing with complex signals that exhibit multiple oscillatory components. This can lead to difficulties in accurately capturing all components of the signal, as non-sinusoidal characteristics can obscure the fundamental frequencies.

### Overfitting Models

Daubechies emphasizes the risk of overfitting when using cepstrum analysis. When models include too many parameters to fit the data, they can replicate noise rather than revealing meaningful signal components. This overfitting can result in analyses that seem to align perfectly with the historical data but fail to generalize well to new data, limiting the practical applicability of the results.

### Computational Complexity

The computational demands of cepstrum-based methods may also present significant challenges. As the complexity of the signal increases, the processing required to analyze and extract meaningful information can become quite resource-intensive. This can limit the feasibility of real-time applications in environments where quick decision-making is essential, such as in medical monitoring or industrial applications.

### Interpretation Difficulties

Lastly, Daubechies highlights interpretation challenges associated with cepstrum outputs. The cepstrum provides a representation of the signal that can be less intuitive compared to other methods like Fourier analysis. This requires practitioners to have a deeper understanding of the mathematical underpinnings to accurately interpret the results, which can act as a barrier for those less familiar with such techniques.

### Conclusion

These limitations collectively highlight the need for continual refinement in methods for signal analysis, especially in the face of complex real-world signals. The challenges presented by non-sinusoidal waveforms, the risk of overfitting, computational requirements, and difficulties in interpretation underscore the importance of developing adaptive models that can better address the intricacies of signal extraction. Daubechies' critiques and insights reflect a nuanced understanding of cepstrum methodologies and signal processing, advocating for mindful application and ongoing innovation in this critical area of research.
