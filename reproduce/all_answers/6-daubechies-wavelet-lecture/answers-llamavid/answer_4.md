Time-frequency representations are mathematical structures that describe the frequency content of signals over time. They are important for applications like speech recognition and compression.


The key mathematical structures underpinning time-frequency representations are the Fourier transform and its inverse transform. The Fourier transform is an operator that maps a function from the real line to the frequency domain. It decomposes a function into its frequency components and allows us to analyze signals in terms of their frequency content.


The inverse Fourier transform is a reverse process that reconstructs a signal from its frequency components. It maps frequency components back to the time domain.


Together, the Fourier transform and inverse Fourier transform allow us to represent signals as a superposition of their frequency components. This decomposition is useful for tasks like time-frequency localization and compression.


In summary, the Fourier transform and inverse Fourier transform are the key mathematical structures that underpin time-frequency representations. They allow us to analyze signals in terms of their frequency content, which is important for applications like speech recognition and compression.
