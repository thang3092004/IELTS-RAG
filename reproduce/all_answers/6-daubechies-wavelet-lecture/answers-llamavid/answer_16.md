Daubechies describes the appearance of a spectrogram as a two-dimensional plot of frequency versus time, with a series of overlapping "narrow" spectrograms representing frequency components. The spectrograms are constructed by first taking a windowed Fourier transform of the signal and then translating the window to different positions in frequency and time. The frequency range of each spectrogram is determined by the window function. The spectrograms show the frequency content of the signal at each point in time, with the frequency content changing over time as the window is translated. The spectrograms provide a visual representation of how the frequency content of the signal changes over time, with narrow spectrograms representing fast frequency components and wide spectrograms representing slow frequency components. This allows the identification of changes in frequency content over time and the localization of frequency components in time frequency space.
