Xinjiang’s unique identity is shaped by its diverse population and its location on the Silk Road.

*   Xinjiang is home to many ethnic minorities from Central Asia, including the Uyghurs, Kazaks, Kyrgyz, and Tajik people. The Uyghur language is spoken in Xinjiang, and Arabic writing can be seen on signs. The cultural influence of these groups can be seen in the food, clothing, and architecture of the region. For instance, one can find *naan*, a type of grilled bread, in both Xinjiang and Central Asia.
*   Xinjiang’s location on the Silk Road has also contributed to its unique cultural identity. The International Grand Bazaar in Ürümqi, for example, sells a variety of goods that reflect the cultural exchange that took place along the Silk Road, such as dried fruits, colorful textiles, and lamps reminiscent of *Aladdin*. 

The sources describe some specific examples of Xinjiang's unique culture:

*   **Foods**: pomegranate juice, lamb, sheep, *naan*, *samsas*, *kawap*, sunflower seeds in the sunflower, walnuts, raisins, grapes, figs, milk tea. 
*   **Clothing**: brightly colored clothing and cloaks. 
*   **Architecture**: mosques, Chinese temples, the International Grand Bazaar, the Bezeklik Thousand Buddha Caves, the Tuyoq Ancient Village, and buildings with domes. 
*   **Religion**: Buddhism and Islam. 

The sources also note that traveling in Xinjiang can be difficult without a tour guide because there is limited public transportation and much of the signage is in Chinese.
