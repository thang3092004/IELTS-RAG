Saint-Gobain measures the impact of its sustainability initiatives in several ways. They aim to both **maximize the positive impacts of their solutions** and **minimize their own negative environmental footprint**. Here are a few examples of how they accomplish this:

*   **Measuring energy savings:** For renovations using their products, Saint-Gobain tracks things like the percentage reduction in energy bills, which directly translates to reduced CO2 emissions. For example, they highlight a 70% reduction in energy bills achieved through single-family home renovations in France.
*   **Quantifying improvements in well-being:** They go beyond just energy savings and try to document improvements to well-being from their products. For instance, Saint-Gobain has studied the correlation between classroom acoustics and teacher absenteeism, finding that better acoustics lead to a more favorable work environment for teachers and potentially improved learning for students.
*   **Tracking CO2 emissions:** They are committed to being carbon neutral by 2050 and have implemented an internal carbon price to guide investment decisions and minimize their footprint. Their progress is measured by tracking overall CO2 emissions and the carbon intensity per euro of turnover, both of which have decreased significantly over the past few years.
*   **Promoting circular economy practices:** Saint-Gobain actively incorporates recycled materials into its manufacturing processes. They cite examples like glass wool insulation in Europe containing 70% recycled materials and a plasterboard product with 50% recycled content.

By employing these diverse metrics, Saint-Gobain aims to demonstrate the tangible benefits of their sustainability efforts, not just for the environment but also for the well-being of individuals and the economic performance of their business.
