Anthropomorphic Test Devices (ATDs), commonly known as crash test dummies, play a critical role in injury biomechanics research. **Because it is unethical to subject humans to potentially injurious events, researchers use ATDs as surrogates to understand how the human body responds to forces in a crash.** ATDs are sophisticated instruments designed to mimic the human response at the whole-body level.

ATDs are equipped with:

*  triaxial accelerometers in the head 
*  axial load sensors in the femurs 
*  chest compression sensors 
*  accelerometers in the neck

Over many years, researchers have studied and learned how to correlate sensor measurements from the ATDs to the actual risk of injury to humans. Researchers used data from:

* cadaver testing 
*  animal testing 
* non-injurious testing

to develop these correlations. 

**Using data from ATDs, researchers can perform injury risk analyses to predict the probability of a human sustaining a serious injury in a particular crash scenario.** For example, the Head Injury Criterion (HIC) is a resultant measurement from the linear acceleration versus time curve that correlates to the severity of a brain injury. This metric takes into account the magnitude, duration, and shape of the acceleration curve. **Researchers can plot the risk of serious injury for a variety of injuries (e.g. brain injury, neck injury, femur fracture) by using data from ATDs along with established injury risk curves.**

In the United States, the Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards require manufacturers to use ATDs to prove their vehicles’ injury metrics are below the threshold likely to cause serious injury.
