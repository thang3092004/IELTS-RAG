The concept of **injury tolerance** refers to the ability of biological tissues to withstand forces and stresses without sustaining damage or harm. Understanding injury tolerance is crucial for developing effective safety measures and treatments for injuries. The sources discuss a variety of methods used to determine human tolerance limits, including:

* **Injurious testing**: This involves controlled experiments where forces are applied to cadaver tissue or animals to observe the resulting injuries. While these studies can provide valuable insights into injury mechanisms, ethical considerations limit their use with live human subjects. 

* **The use of Anthropomorphic Test Devices (ATDs) or crash test dummies**: These sophisticated instruments are designed to mimic human responses to impact forces and are equipped with sensors to measure various parameters like acceleration and force. These dummies are extensively used in automotive safety research to assess injury risk in crash scenarios.

* **Real-world accident data analysis**:  Researchers utilize databases containing information from real-world accidents, including vehicle speeds, seatbelt usage, and injury outcomes. Analyzing this data helps identify correlations and causal factors that contribute to injury risk. However, the lack of control over variables in real-world scenarios makes it difficult to isolate specific causes and effects. 

* **Non-injurious testing**: This method involves applying forces to human volunteers within safe limits and monitoring their responses. Examples include measuring head acceleration during soccer ball heading or using sensors in football helmets to assess concussion risk. 

These different methods provide researchers with a comprehensive understanding of human tolerance limits for various types of injuries.  By studying how biological tissues respond to forces, scientists and engineers can develop safety measures to prevent injuries and design medical interventions to treat them effectively.
