Injury tolerance refers to the ability of the human body to withstand and recover from injury. Engineers and biomechanical engineers use a variety of methods to understand and model human injury tolerance, which helps them design safer products and systems. 

One method is to study real-world accidents and injuries to understand how the human body responds to different levels of trauma. They can analyze the injuries sustained in a crash, like the one shown in the video, to gain insights into the mechanics of the injury. This helps them determine how much force and displacement the human body can tolerate before sustaining serious damage. 

Another method is to study how the human body responds to different types of forces and motions in a simulated environment. They can use dummies with sensors to measure the forces and motions applied to the body, and then correlate the injury risk to the forces and motions. This allows them to determine injury risk for different types of accidents and vehicle motions. 

Engineers can also study how the human body responds to injury over time. They can observe how injuries heal and recover, and how the body adapts to new forces and motions over time. This helps them design restraint systems and protective devices that can withstand repeated impacts.

In summary, engineers and biomechanical engineers use various methods to study human injury tolerance, from analyzing real accidents to simulating and studying the body's response over time, in order to design safer products and systems.
