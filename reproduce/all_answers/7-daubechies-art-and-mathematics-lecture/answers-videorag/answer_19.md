The North Carolina Museum of Art (NCMA) expressed several concerns regarding the reconstruction of the Gisi Altarpiece, largely revolving around the integrity and completeness of the artwork. Initially, one of the main issues identified was that although many of the altarpiece's panels were present and located in different museums across the United States, one particular panel was missing. This absence raised doubts about the feasibility of presenting a truly unified piece, as one museum suggested that even if they had the majority of panels, the incomplete set detracted from the overall authenticity of the reconstruction.

Additionally, there were apprehensions related to the historical significance of the panels and their provenance, especially given that the altarpiece had been disassembled, sold, and scattered among various collections over the years. The NCMA faced the challenge of ensuring that any reconstruction adhered to the original artistic intent while also capturing the history and context from which the altarpiece emerged. 

The museum's commitment to collaborative efforts to preserve and display this significant art piece was also articulated, as they sought to reunite its various components for the first time in a scholarly and museum-quality exhibit. Overall, their concerns underscored a desire for authenticity and completeness in presenting the Gisi Altarpiece, reflecting broader issues related to art conservation and restoration practices.
