In her presentations, Ingrid Daubechies discussed various applications of mathematics in the fields of art conservation and scientific research apart from her collaboration with the North Carolina Museum of Art. Here’s a summary highlighting some key areas she focused on:

### Mathematical Tools for Art Conservation

1. **Image Analysis**: Daubechies emphasized the importance of mathematical tools in image processing, particularly in the context of art conservation. She mentioned how image analysis has become integral to identifying and restoring artworks, allowing conservators to distinguish between original pieces and reproductions.

2. **Automatic Thread Counting**: Another application highlighted was the work of electrical engineer Rick Johnson, who developed automatic thread counting techniques for canvases. This method allows for detailed analysis of paintings, aiding conservators in determining the composition and authenticity of artworks by automating what previously would have been a manual and time-consuming task.

3. **Virtual Aging/Rejuvenation of Paintings**: In connection with her research efforts, Daubechies mentioned projects that involve virtually aging or rejuvenating artwork. This application of mathematics helps analyze how artworks have changed over time and can assist in the planning of restoration techniques.

4. **Collaborations with Art Historians**: As she pointed out, mathematicians like herself work alongside art historians and conservators to apply mathematical principles to various challenges within art conservation. This interdisciplinary approach highlights the significant role of mathematics in understanding art’s history, technique, and preservation methods.

### Broader Impact

Daubechies emphasized that the mathematical contributions to art conservation are not just limited to technical applications but also involve considerable theoretical insights, allowing for a deeper understanding of artistic techniques and materials. This interplay between mathematics and the arts fosters innovation and collaboration among fields that traditionally operate separately.

Overall, her discussions illustrated a clear convergence of mathematical techniques with practical needs in the realm of art preservation, showcasing how such interdisciplinary approaches can enhance both scientific research and cultural heritage.
