Ingrid Daubechies, a renowned mathematician known for her work in wavelets and image processing, has engaged in significant collaborations with the North Carolina Museum of Art. This partnership exemplifies the intersection of mathematics and art, showcasing how mathematical principles can enhance art conservation and historical analysis.

### Ghissi Project

One of the notable ventures is the **Gizzy Project**, where Daubechies and her colleagues explored virtual aging and rejuvenation techniques for paintings. This project involved a collaboration between Duke University and the museum, focusing on paintings crafted on panel. The aim was to apply mathematical algorithms to restore and enhance the visual qualities of artworks, thereby enabling art historians and conservators to analyze and understand these pieces more effectively.

### Art Conservation Techniques

Daubechies's work has also highlighted the application of mathematical algorithms in art conservation. In several lectures, she discussed how these algorithms assist conservators in piecing together fragmented artworks, such as frescoes damaged during historical events. For instance, they used mathematical techniques to help reconstruct many pieces of a famous fresco destroyed during World War II, demonstrating the value of mathematics in restoring cultural heritage.

### Collaborative Research and Outcomes

Throughout her collaboration, Daubechies has emphasized the importance of interdisciplinary research. By bringing mathematicians and art experts together, they tackled complex challenges in art restoration. Young researchers and students, like Rachel Yin and Bruno Cornelis, have been involved in these projects, showcasing how students benefit from collaborating with experts in both fields.

The combined efforts of Daubechies and the North Carolina Museum of Art not only aim to preserve art but also to explore its mathematical underpinnings, thus fostering a better understanding of both disciplines. The ongoing dialogue between mathematics and art continues to generate innovative solutions in the fields of art restoration and historical documentation.

### Conclusion

Ingrid Daubechies's collaboration with the North Carolina Museum of Art serves as an inspiring model of how interdisciplinary partnerships can lead to meaningful advancements in conservation efforts. By integrating mathematical principles into the analysis and preservation of artwork, this partnership not only contributes to the art historical field but also emphasizes the broader implications of mathematical applications in cultural heritage.
