Mathematics played a crucial role in the reconstruction of the frescoes in Padua, particularly those damaged during World War II. The process involved a blend of mathematical techniques and advanced technology to effectively analyze and restore these historical artworks.

### 1. Decomposition into Circular Harmonics
One of the significant mathematical methods employed was the decomposition of images into circular harmonics. This technique allowed researchers and conservators to determine if one image was a rotated version of another by checking the proportionalities of their coefficients. If a rotated image (Rot) could be expressed in terms of a linear combination of several original images (Im), the relationship could be swiftly verified. This mathematical underpinning enabled the efficient handling of numerous fragments, aiding in their correct placement during the reconstruction process.

### 2. The Mantegna Project
The Mantegna Project, which commenced in 2001, involved over 50 volunteers collaborating across various disciplines, including mathematics, to reconstruct the frescoes. Volunteers utilized computers to analyze thousands of digital fragments. By applying algorithms, the project aimed to match these fragments to the original artwork. The mathematical models enabled the volunteers to quickly identify possible matches, producing a list of the best candidates for each fragment's placement.

### 3. Numerical Computation and Visual Analysis
The workflow at MantLab, established in Padua for this restoration effort, included three main phases: fragment selection, numerical computation, and visual analysis. After selecting fragments from a digital catalogue, numerical computation involved algorithms that analyzed different rotated versions of the fragments to find the best candidates for restoration. Following this, human operators visually confirmed these matches, ensuring that the final assembly remained true to the artefacts’ historical integrity.

### 4. Digital Tools and Computational Inpainting
Further enhancing the accuracy of the reconstruction was the application of digital tools and computational inpainting techniques. These methods allowed conservators to mathematically model and fill in missing parts of the artwork based on the color and texture data available from the existing fragments. This not only facilitated the restoration but also provided a way to visualize how the completed frescoes would have looked before their damage.

### Conclusion
In summary, mathematics significantly facilitated the reconstruction of the Padua frescoes through advanced algorithms and techniques that enabled rapid analysis and matching of fragments, ensuring authenticity and historical accuracy in the restoration process. The combination of mathematical precision with artistry underscores the interdisciplinary nature of art restoration efforts, leading to a successful revival of these important cultural artifacts.
