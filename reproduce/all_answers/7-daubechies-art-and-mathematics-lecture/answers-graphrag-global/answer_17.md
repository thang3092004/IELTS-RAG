Ingrid Daubechies has made significant contributions in applying mathematical techniques to art conservation and scientific research, extending well beyond her work with the North Carolina Museum of Art. Her interdisciplinary approach bridges the gap between mathematics, art, and biology, showcasing various applications that enhance both conservation strategies and scientific analysis.

### Mathematical Techniques in Art Conservation

Daubechies has emphasized the use of advanced mathematical methodologies such as wavelet theory and diffusion maps in art preservation. These techniques facilitate the analysis of artworks, enabling conservators to better understand local geometries and structural integrity. For instance, wavelet transforms have found applications in digital image processing, helping to represent artworks accurately and aiding in their restoration. This aspect is crucial in maintaining the quality and authenticity of artworks during conservation efforts.

Additionally, Daubechies has discussed the integration of statistical methods to transform the approach of art historians and conservators. By utilizing mathematical models, conservators can systematically analyze artworks, ensuring their preservation strategies are both effective and scientifically grounded. This application is further supported by her collaboration in projects such as the Mantegna Project, where mathematical decomposition methods are employed by volunteers to enhance art restoration processes.

### Interdisciplinary Research and Educational Initiatives

Beyond art conservation, Daubechies is involved in innovative research that merges mathematics with biological understanding, highlighting the significant overlap between these fields. Her work on machine learning and fiber bundles illustrates how mathematical principles can be applied not only to artistic analysis but also to biological mapping, thereby enriching both areas of study.

Daubechies has also organized events that focus on the educational outreach of these disciplines. Initiatives like Mathematomy and Mathemalchemy aim to inspire future generations by showing the creative applications of mathematics within art and conservation, thus promoting mathematical literacy. Furthermore, her presentations, such as those in the Green Family Lecture Series at Duke University, underline her commitment to fostering dialogue among scholars from different fields, enhancing the understanding of how mathematics can be creatively integrated into artistic practices.
