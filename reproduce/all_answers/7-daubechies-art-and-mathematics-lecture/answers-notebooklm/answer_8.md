Massimo Fonasier developed a method to reconstruct damaged frescoes in the Eremitani Church in Padua that were destroyed during World War II.  The church’s frescoes, including works by Mantegna, were reduced to rubble and stored in boxes for decades. In the 1990s, a project was started to reconstruct the frescoes digitally using the surviving fragments. 

The project involved: 

*   Cleaning, stabilizing, numbering, and digitizing over 80,000 fresco fragments. 
*   Creating full-scale printouts of the original frescoes from high-resolution black and white photos taken before the war.

Fonasier's reconstruction method used **circular harmonics** to digitally piece the fresco fragments back together.

*   **Circular harmonics** are special mathematical functions that are easily manipulated using rotations.
*   Each fresco fragment containing distinguishing features was fitted within the largest possible circle.
*   This circular image was decomposed into circular harmonics.
*   This decomposition allowed the researchers to easily rotate the fragments to find their correct placement. 

Volunteers at a computer lab in Padua used Fonasier’s program to find the placement of each fragment. The program analyzed the circular harmonic decompositions of the fragments and compared them to the pre-computed tables of circular harmonic decompositions for the full-scale fresco printouts. The program would suggest several possible locations for a given fragment, and the volunteer would ultimately decide the correct placement. After a fragment’s placement was chosen, art historians would physically place the fragment on the full-size printout.

This process allowed the volunteers and art historians to place all the fresco fragments with content back onto the printouts. This reconstruction provided valuable information for the art conservators, such as the colors of various objects and surfaces in the frescoes, which they then used to create a full-color reconstruction. Fonasier is currently developing a program that would allow visitors to the Eremitani Church to view a vivid color reconstruction of the frescoes on their smartphones.
