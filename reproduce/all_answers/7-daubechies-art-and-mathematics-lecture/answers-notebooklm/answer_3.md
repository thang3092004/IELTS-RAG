X-ray fluorescence was used to analyze a painting by Van Gogh that had another painting underneath. The painting on top was of a patch of grass, and x-rays showed a portrait of a peasant woman underneath.  This intrigued researchers because Van Gogh had written about this painting in a letter to his brother, Theo. They believed that the portrait underneath was the same one that he valued so much before moving to Paris and meeting the Impressionists.

X-ray fluorescence works by using high-energy x-rays to ionize the atoms of the pigments on the canvas. When the atoms fall back to their normal state, they emit light at wavelengths that are specific to each element. Researchers used this information to determine which pigments Van Gogh had used.

**The x-ray fluorescence analysis highlighted the elements arsenic and antimony, which were key to identifying the pigments and reconstructing the colors.** Antimony was a component of Naples yellow, the only light color Van Gogh was thought to have used on his palette.  This helped to create a luminance map. Researchers also used the fluorescence data in combination with an analysis of other portraits from the same period to make inferences about missing colors.  This allowed for a more complete color reconstruction of the portrait under the grass.
