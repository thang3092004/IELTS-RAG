In her talks, Ingrid Daubechies discusses several applications of mathematics to art conservation and scientific research, in addition to her work with the North Carolina Museum of Art. These include:

*   **Reconstruction of destroyed frescoes:** Daubechies describes the work of Massimo Fornasier in reconstructing frescoes in the Eremitani Church in Padua that were destroyed during World War II. Fornasier developed a method to digitally piece together thousands of fresco fragments using circular harmonics to compare fragment shapes. Volunteers helped identify the correct placement of fragments using computer-generated suggestions. The final reconstruction is viewable in the church, and Fornasier is working on a project that would allow visitors to see a digitally colorized version of the fresco on their smartphones.
*   **Automatic thread counting for paintings on canvas:** Daubechies discusses the work of Rick Johnson, who developed an algorithm for automatic thread counting in paintings on canvas. This technique allows art historians to analyze the canvas weave and identify paintings that were created from the same roll of canvas. This information can be used for attribution and authentication purposes.
*   **Analysis of pigments using X-ray fluorescence:** Daubechies describes her own work, along with collaborators, in analyzing the pigments used in a Van Gogh painting using X-ray fluorescence data. This technique provides information about the elemental composition of the pigments, revealing details about the painting that are not visible to the naked eye. By correcting for artifacts in the data and using in-painting techniques, the researchers were able to create a more accurate representation of the painting's original appearance. 
*   **Virtual aging and rejuvenation of paintings:** Daubechies discusses the “Ghisi” project, in which she and her collaborators used image analysis techniques to virtually age a reconstructed panel of a 14th-century altarpiece so that it would match the appearance of the original panels. They were also able to virtually rejuvenate the original panels, providing a glimpse of how the altarpiece might have looked when it was newly created.  This project involved analyzing the crack patterns, color changes, and gilding techniques of the original panels and using mathematical algorithms to reproduce these effects on the reconstructed panel.
*   **Analysis of 3D shapes in biological morphology:** In addition to her work in art conservation, Daubechies also applies her expertise in image analysis and mathematical modeling to scientific research. She discusses her collaborations with biologists who study teeth and bones. This work involves analyzing 3D shapes obtained from CT scans and developing mathematical tools to compare and classify different specimens. Daubechies uses techniques from differential geometry and fiber bundle theory to analyze these shapes and discover underlying patterns.

**Overall, Daubechies' work demonstrates the diverse and powerful applications of mathematics in art conservation and scientific research.** By combining mathematical insights with domain-specific knowledge, she and her collaborators have made significant contributions to our understanding of art, history, and the natural world.
