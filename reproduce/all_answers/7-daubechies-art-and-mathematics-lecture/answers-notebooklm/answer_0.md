Mathematical tools can significantly enhance art historical analysis in various ways, as demonstrated by the examples discussed in the sources:

**Reconstruction of Damaged Artwork:**  The use of **circular harmonics** allowed for the efficient reconstruction of frescoes in the Eremitani Church in Padua that were destroyed during World War II. These functions, which have specific properties under rotation, were used to decompose images of fresco fragments, enabling researchers to rapidly identify their placement within the larger composition.

**Canvas Analysis and Painting Attribution:** Automatic thread counting, using Fourier transforms to analyze the periodicity of canvas weaves in X-ray images, provided a "fingerprint" of canvases. This has helped identify paintings created using canvas from the same roll ("roll mates") and has even contributed to the reattribution of paintings previously thought to be by students or imitators. 

**Revealing Hidden Details and Virtual Restoration:**  Advanced imaging techniques like **X-ray fluorescence**, combined with mathematical tools for image processing and analysis, such as **in-painting** and **denoising**, have allowed researchers to reveal hidden details beneath the surface of paintings. This information is crucial for understanding an artist's process and for making informed decisions about conservation and restoration.  Furthermore, these tools allow for the creation of **virtual restorations**, providing insights into how a painting might have looked originally without physically altering the artwork.

**Style Analysis and Connoisseurship:** Wavelet decomposition, a mathematical technique for analyzing images at different scales and orientations, was used to statistically characterize an artist's style. This approach has the potential to assist in attributing paintings based on quantitative analysis of stylistic features.

**Digital Humanities and the Future of Art History:**  The successful application of mathematical tools in art conservation and analysis has spurred interest in applying these techniques more broadly in the field of art history. For example, researchers are exploring the use of shape recognition algorithms to identify recurring motifs and compositions across different artworks. This integration of mathematical and computational methods promises to enrich art historical research by providing new insights and avenues for exploration.

The sources highlight the collaborative nature of these projects, emphasizing the importance of communication and understanding between mathematicians and art historians.  By combining their expertise, they can unlock new information and deepen our appreciation for art.
