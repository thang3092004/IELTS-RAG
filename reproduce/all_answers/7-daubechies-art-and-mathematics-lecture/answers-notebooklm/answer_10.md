The sources emphasize that while mathematics plays a crucial role in art conservation, **human expertise remains essential**. Mathematical tools provide information and insights, but **art historians and conservators ultimately make decisions based on their knowledge and experience.**

Here are some specific examples from the sources:

* **Reconstruction of the Eremitani Church frescoes:** While Massimo Fonasier’s algorithm could identify potential matches for fresco fragments, human volunteers reviewed the suggestions and made the final decisions. This ensured accuracy and integrated the art historians’ knowledge of the frescoes' content and style.
* **Thread counting in Van Gogh paintings:** Automatic thread counting algorithms provide quantitative data that would be tedious and time-consuming to gather manually.  However, art historians utilize this data to interpret patterns and make attributions based on their understanding of canvas production and artists' practices.
* **Virtual aging of the Ghissi altarpiece panel:** The virtual aging process involved multiple steps, including color remapping, crack simulation, and gold leaf aging. Art conservators worked closely with mathematicians, providing input on the accuracy of colors and crack patterns to ensure a historically informed and visually convincing outcome.
* **Cracks removal from Van Eyck’s Ghent Altarpiece:** The algorithm successfully removed cracks from the minuscule text in Van Eyck's painting, but it was ultimately the expertise of art historians who deciphered the text and identified its significance. 

The sources repeatedly emphasize that **mathematical tools enhance, rather than replace, human expertise**. Collaboration between mathematicians and art historians leads to a richer understanding and more effective conservation efforts.
