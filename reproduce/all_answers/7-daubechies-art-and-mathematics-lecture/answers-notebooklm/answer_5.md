Ingrid Daubechies collaborated with the North Carolina Museum of Art (NCMA) on a project called the **Gisi Project**, which focused on a 14th-century altarpiece by an unknown artist. The NCMA had three panels of the altarpiece, but one panel was missing.

Here is a detailed account of the collaboration:

* **Reconstruction of the missing panel**: Daubechies connected the NCMA with Charlotte Caspers, an artist and art conservator who specializes in art reconstruction. Caspers researched the altarpiece and designed a reconstruction of the missing panel using elements and the painting style of the existing panels. 
* **Virtual aging of the new panel**: The reconstructed panel was vibrant and new looking, which contrasted too strongly with the existing panels. Daubechies and her team used image analysis and processing techniques to **virtually age** the new panel, making it look more consistent with the existing ones. This involved:
    * Remapping the colors of the new panel to match the aged colors of the old panels.
    * Analyzing and replicating the crack patterns found on the old panels.
    * Recreating the aged appearance of the gold leaf.
* **Virtual rejuvenation of the existing panels**: The team also used the color mapping data to create virtually rejuvenated versions of the existing panels, showcasing what they would have looked like when new.
* **Cradle removal**: Daubechies and her team developed an algorithm to **automatically remove the visual artifacts of cradles** from X-rays of paintings. This algorithm was made available to art conservators through a Photoshop plugin and is accessible at projectplatypus.net.
* **Digital aspects of the exhibition**: The Gisi Project exhibition included a documentary of the reconstruction process, materials used, and a digital wall explaining the role of image processing and mathematics.

The collaboration was a success, drawing a large audience and demonstrating the value of mathematics in art conservation and history.
