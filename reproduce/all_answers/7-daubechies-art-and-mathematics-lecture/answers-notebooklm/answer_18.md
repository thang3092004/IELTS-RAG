Ingrid Daubechies's work has significantly influenced the practices of art conservators and historians by providing them with new tools and techniques to analyze, restore, and understand works of art. Here are some key examples of her influence:

* **Reconstruction of Damaged Frescoes:** Daubechies's former postdoc, Massimo Fornasier, utilized **circular harmonics** to develop an algorithm that helped art historians reconstruct frescoes in the Eremitani Church in Padua that were destroyed during World War II. This algorithm efficiently compared small fragments of the frescoes to black-and-white photographs, allowing for the accurate placement of thousands of pieces. This mathematical approach revolutionized the reconstruction process, making it faster and more precise.

* **Automatic Thread Counting and Canvas Characterization:** Daubechies and electrical engineer Rick Johnson developed a method for **automatically counting threads in canvases using the Fourier transform**. This technique analyzes the periodicity of threads visible in X-rays, which are caused by variations in the thickness of lead white primer applied to the canvas. This information provides a "fingerprint" of the canvas, allowing art historians to: 
    * Characterize canvases more efficiently and accurately. 
    * Determine if paintings were created using canvas from the same roll ("role mates"). 
    * Re-attribute paintings based on canvas analysis. This technique has been instrumental in re-attributing works by artists such as Van Gogh and Velázquez.

* **Virtual Aging and Rejuvenation of Paintings:** Daubechies and her team developed techniques for **virtually aging and rejuvenating paintings**, which was showcased in the "Gisi Reunited" project. These techniques involved:
    * **Color remapping:** Analyzing how pigments age and creating algorithms to simulate those changes.
    * **Crack simulation:** Studying crack patterns in old paintings and developing algorithms to reproduce them digitally. 
    * **Gold leaf rendering:** Simulating the reflective properties of gold leaf by reconstructing the artwork as a 3D object. This approach provides a more realistic representation of the gilded surfaces compared to traditional photographs.

* **Revealing Hidden Details and Removing Artifacts:** Daubechies and her team have developed algorithms for:
    * **Inpainting:** Filling in areas of missing information in X-rays caused by impasto or other obstructions.
    * **Removing cradle marks:** Removing the distracting marks of wooden cradles visible in X-rays of paintings on panels. These algorithms allow art historians and conservators to better visualize and analyze underlying details in paintings. 

Daubechies's work demonstrates how **mathematical tools and techniques can be powerful assets for art conservation and historical research**. Her collaborations with museums and art experts have led to the development of innovative methods that enhance our understanding of artworks and aid in their preservation. Her work also highlights the **interdisciplinary nature of art conservation and the importance of collaboration between scientists and art experts**.
