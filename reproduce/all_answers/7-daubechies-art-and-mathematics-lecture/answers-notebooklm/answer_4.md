The bombing of the Eremitani Church in Padua during World War II resulted in the destruction of frescoes by Mantegna and other renowned painters. While high-resolution black-and-white pictures and the least-damaged frescoes survived, the rest were reduced to rubble, creating a daunting reconstruction task. In the 1990s, with advancements in computational power, a project was initiated to digitize and analyze the fresco fragments, seeking a solution for this enormous puzzle. Massimo Fonasier developed a mathematical approach using **circular harmonics** to reconstruct the frescoes.

Here's how mathematical techniques aided in the reconstruction:

- **Image Digitization and Representation:** Images were digitized by dividing them into tiny squares called pixels. Each pixel’s gray level was represented by a number from 0 (black) to 255 (white), enabling computational analysis.
- **Circular Harmonics for Rotation:** Circular harmonics are special functions with easily calculable rotation properties. By decomposing each fresco fragment’s image into circular harmonics, the algorithm could quickly compute the representation of its rotated versions.
- **Efficient Matching Algorithm:** Using the circular harmonic decomposition, a fast algorithm was developed to compare fresco fragments with the high-resolution black-and-white images of the original frescoes. By analyzing different rotations of each fragment, the algorithm identified potential locations on the fresco.
- **Human-Computer Collaboration:** A computer lab was set up in Padua where volunteers analyzed the algorithm's suggestions for fragment placement. The volunteers selected the best match from the top choices presented by the algorithm, ensuring accuracy and incorporating human judgment.

This approach allowed for the successful placement of the majority of fresco fragments onto a full-scale printout of the original frescoes. The placement not only revealed the overall composition but also provided crucial information about colors, enabling art conservators to reconstruct the frescoes. Today, visitors to the Eremitani Church can see the reconstructed frescoes, with pastel colors indicating the inpainted areas. Massimo Fonasier is currently working on a project to provide visitors with a virtual coloration of the frescoes accessible via smartphones, further enhancing their appreciation of the artwork.
