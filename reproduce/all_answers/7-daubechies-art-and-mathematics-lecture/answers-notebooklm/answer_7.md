To virtually age the ninth panel of the Giotto altarpiece, researchers needed to:

*   **Remap the new colors to old versions.** This was done pigment by pigment field because each pigment ages differently.  Charlotte, the artist who repainted the panel, used the same pigments as the original artist after they were chemically analyzed.  Researchers were able to study how the pigments changed over time. 
*   **Put in cracks**. Researchers studied the “crackle-ooh” or the pattern of cracks in the original panels. They had experience with automatically detecting cracks from previous projects, including one involving a painting by Van Dyck. 
*   **Age the gold leaf work.** The gold leaf on the original panels was aged, so researchers needed to make the gold on the new panel look older as well.

Researchers also rejuvenated the eight original panels by:

*   **Removing the cracks**.
*   **Remapping the old colors to new versions.** This was done using the inverse of the process used to age the ninth panel.
*   **Rejuvenating the gold work.** This involved creating a 3D model of the panel and rendering the reflections of the gold leaf, taking into account that the gold was punched with a hammer.

The aged ninth panel was printed in high resolution and exhibited with the original panels.  The rejuvenated panels were displayed on a screen.
