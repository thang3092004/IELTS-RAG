The North Carolina Museum of Art (NCMA) had multiple concerns regarding the reconstruction of the Ghissi Altarpiece, particularly centered around its historical completeness and authenticity. One major concern was the fact that one of the panels from the original altarpiece was missing. The absence of this panel raised questions about the integrity and completeness of the altarpiece as a whole since it was only discovered later that these paintings belonged together as part of a single altarpiece.

Furthermore, the NCMA faced challenges in uniting all the dispersed panels that resided across various museums, including the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York City and the Art Institute in Chicago. Because the missing piece was never found, there was reluctance among other institutions to facilitate the reunification, as they believed an incomplete set would not fully represent the original artwork. Institutions expressed that even if all known panels were gathered, the absence of the missing piece would still diminish the significance of the display.

Additionally, the NCMA had logistical concerns regarding cross-border cooperation and transportation. Despite most of the artworks being within the United States, there were still complexities surrounding the borrowing of artworks from different museums, which added to the debates about whether or not it would be worthwhile to attempt the reunification of the panels given that one was permanently missing.

Overall, these concerns highlighted the complexities involved in art reconstruction, emphasizing the delicate balance between artistic interpretation and historical fidelity in presenting historical artworks.
