### Applications of Mathematics in Art Conservation and Scientific Research

In her presentations, Professor Ingrid Daubechies highlighted various significant applications of mathematics to the fields of art conservation and scientific research. Her discussions centered around innovative projects and methodologies that utilize mathematical principles to address challenges within art history and conservation efforts.

#### 1. **Virtual Aging and Rejuvenation of Paintings**
One of the notable projects mentioned was the **Ghissi Project**, which involved collaboration with Duke University alongside the North Carolina Museum of Art. The project focuses on virtual aging and rejuvenating paintings on panel, providing a mathematical framework to analyze and restore artworks through technological means.

#### 2. **Image Analysis for Art Conservation**
Daubechies pointed out that mathematics, especially through image analysis techniques, is essential in conserving artworks. This includes methods for reconstructing damaged frescoes, such as those in the Eremitani Church in Padua, Italy, which were damaged during World War II. The use of algorithms allows conservators to piece together thousands of fragments, which otherwise would remain of limited use without such mathematical grounding.

#### 3. **Collaboration with Material Scientists**
She also emphasized the interdisciplinary nature of her work, describing how collaborations with material scientists like Joris Dik and Koen Janssen led to significant advancements in understanding the materials used in artworks. Their joint efforts aim to analyze the authenticity and provenance of artworks, such as the paintings attributed to Van Gogh, combining statistical analysis and interpretation of visual data.

#### 4. **Enhancements to Art Historical Methodologies**
Daubechies explored the broader implications of mathematical algorithms in art. This included addressing stylistic variations within master paintings by conducting statistical analyses across multiple paintings by the same artist, which helps identify attributes that can distinguish authentic works from forgeries and copies.

### Conclusion
Throughout her discussions, Daubechies illustrated the valuable intersection of mathematics and art conservation. The application of mathematical techniques extends beyond mere restoration; it fundamentally reshapes how art historians and conservators understand, interpret, and preserve cultural heritage. By integrating scientific methodologies and collaborative practices, the discipline continues to evolve, facilitated by the insights of mathematicians.
