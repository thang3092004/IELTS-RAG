## Addressing 'Cradle' Interference in X-Ray Images

Daubechies's team approached the challenge of 'cradle' interference in X-Ray imaging of panel paintings by leveraging advanced image analysis techniques. Cradles are supports attached to the back of artworks that help maintain stability, but they can complicate the X-ray imaging process by obscuring visibility of the panel itself. To overcome this interference, the team utilized high-energy X-ray technology which enables clearer visualization of the underlying structures of artworks while minimizing the impact of the cradle. This method allows for a non-invasive assessment of the artwork's condition without requiring removal of the cradle, a significant concern in art conservation where minimizing intervention is paramount.

## Impact of the Work

The impact of this work is substantial for both art conservation and research. By improving the clarity of X-ray images despite the presence of cradles, Daubechies’s advancements facilitated better analysis of panel paintings. This means conservators can identify issues such as structural damage, hidden layers, or previous restoration efforts with greater accuracy. Moreover, the integration of these mathematical and computational techniques into art conservation practices signifies a progressive step forward in the field, enhancing the understanding of historical artworks while simultaneously preserving them for future generations. Overall, the research not only advances technological applications in art analysis but also fosters greater interdisciplinary collaboration between mathematicians, scientists, and art conservators.
