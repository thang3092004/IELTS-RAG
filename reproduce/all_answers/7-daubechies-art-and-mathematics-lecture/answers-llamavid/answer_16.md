Daubechies's team faced the challenge of interference from the gold-leaf cradles present in panel paintings when scanning X-ray images. The cradles interfered with the X-ray signals, making it difficult to distinguish the underlying painting from the cradle. 

To address this, they developed a virtual cradle removal tool called Platypus. This allowed them to isolate the painting image from the cradle interference.

The impact of this work was significant. It enabled them to reconstruct missing parts of paintings like the Ghent Altarpiece, which had been separated from its cradle over time. This was crucial for reconstructing the painting's original appearance.

Their virtual cradle removal tool also allowed them to identify which cradles were present in the X-ray scans of the Kress Altarpiece. This was important for attributing the painting to the correct artist.

So in summary, Daubechies's team's work in removing virtual cradle interference from X-ray images enabled them to reconstruct missing parts of paintings and identify cradles present in the scans. This had a major impact on the field of art and science collaboration.
