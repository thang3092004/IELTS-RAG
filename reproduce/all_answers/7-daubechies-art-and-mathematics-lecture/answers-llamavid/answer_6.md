Mathematics played a crucial role in reconstructing the Padua frescoes, as seen in the Van Gogh Museum's "Reunited" project. The project aimed to determine which of the many copies of the altarpiece were painted by Van Gogh himself. To do this, the researchers used mathematical techniques to analyze the differences between the different copies. They found that the different copies had different variations in density and weave, which could be represented by circular harmonics. By deconstructing the images into circular harmonics and comparing coefficients, they were able to determine which copies were likely painted by Van Gogh and which were not. This mathematical analysis allowed the researchers to confidently attribute the copies to Van Gogh, despite the lack of direct evidence. The project showed how mathematics can be used to unlock hidden meaning in artwork, even when the art itself is damaged and fragmented.
