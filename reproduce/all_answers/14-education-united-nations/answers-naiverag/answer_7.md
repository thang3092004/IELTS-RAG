## Challenges to Girls' Access to Secondary Education in Afghanistan

Girls in Afghanistan face a myriad of challenges in accessing secondary education, particularly following the changes in governance and the restrictions imposed by the Taliban since 2021. One of the most significant barriers is the outright ban on secondary education for girls announced in March 2022. This policy has forcibly excluded an entire cohort of adolescent girls from furthering their education, severely limiting their future prospects. The impact of this ban is exacerbated by cultural attitudes that often devalue female education and the socio-economic hardships that families face, which can result in prioritizing boys' education over girls'.

The lack of safe educational infrastructures is another critical issue. Many schools, especially in rural areas, are poorly equipped and may not be accessible or secure for girls. Additionally, prevalent societal norms and fears surrounding gender-based violence contribute to parents' hesitance in allowing their daughters to attend school, particularly at the secondary level where longer distances are often required to reach educational institutions. Moreover, the ongoing political instability and insecurity in Afghanistan create an environment where both students and teachers feel unsafe, further hindering educational opportunities for girls.

## Education Cannot Wait's Response

In response to these daunting challenges, Education Cannot Wait (ECW), a global fund dedicated to education in emergencies, has been active in Afghanistan to support educational initiatives, particularly focusing on girls. ECW works through partnerships with civil society organizations and UN agencies, such as UNICEF, to implement education programs that remain operational despite the challenging constraints.

One of the key strategies employed by ECW involves advocating for the right to education and facilitating dialogue with various stakeholders, including those within the Taliban, to promote educational access for girls. For example, ECW has highlighted that not all individuals within the Taliban might oppose girls' education, and there are voices advocating for educational opportunities for their daughters. This indicates potential openings for negotiation and engagement.

Additionally, Education Cannot Wait supports initiatives that maintain educational investments at the local level, ensuring that programs designed to facilitate learning for girls continue despite restrictions. They also focus on creating awareness regarding the importance of education by engaging families and community leaders to change cultural perceptions around girls' schooling. Through these multifaceted approaches, ECW seeks not just to provide immediate educational support but also to foster an environment conducive to sustainable educational reforms in Afghanistan.

In conclusion, while significant challenges remain for girls seeking secondary education in Afghanistan, initiatives by organizations like Education Cannot Wait play a crucial role in addressing these barriers and advocating for a more inclusive educational landscape.
