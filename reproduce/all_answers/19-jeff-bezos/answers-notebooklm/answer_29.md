Jeff Bezos believes that **“wandering” is a key component of innovation and problem-solving**. He has applied this concept to his work at both Amazon and Blue Origin. 

Bezos defines “wandering” as **a willingness to explore different ideas and approaches, even if they seem inefficient at first**. He believes that this type of thinking is essential for coming up with truly innovative solutions, as opposed to incremental improvements. 

Bezos applies the concept of “wandering” in a number of ways. For example:

*   He **encourages his teams to have “messy” meetings** where they brainstorm ideas and challenge each other’s assumptions. He believes that this is more effective than having “rehearsed” meetings where everyone agrees on the best course of action in advance.
*   He **allows his teams to take “appropriate technology risks”** and make decisions quickly. He is willing to change course if necessary, but he believes that it is important to move quickly and decisively.
*   He **invests in a variety of different companies and projects**, even if they seem risky. He believes that this is the best way to find new and innovative ideas.

Bezos acknowledges that **“wandering” can be frustrating at times**. It can lead to dead ends and wasted effort. However, he believes that **the rewards of innovation outweigh the risks**. He is convinced that the most important decisions in business and life are made with instinct, intuition, and “heart,” rather than analysis. Bezos is confident that, in the long run, “wandering” will lead to success. He attributes much of Amazon’s success to its “obsessive-compulsive focus on the customer,” a focus that often requires “wandering” to discover what customers really want.

Bezos’s belief in the power of “wandering” is evident in his personal life as well. He describes himself as a “Wanderer” who is always curious and exploring new things. He says that he gets energy from this process and that it has helped him to be successful in his career.
