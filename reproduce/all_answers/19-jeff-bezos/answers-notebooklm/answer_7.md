Jeff Bezos believes that **the most important decisions in life and business are made with instinct, intuition, taste, and heart**. He attributes this belief to an experience he had in college while struggling with a physics problem with a friend. They went to a classmate, Yosanta, who was considered brilliant, for help. After looking at it briefly, Yosanta said the answer was cosine. When asked if he had done the calculation in his head, Yosanta explained that he had solved a similar problem years ago and was able to map the current problem onto it. Bezos realized in that moment that he would never be a great theoretical physicist. He believes that this type of intuitive problem-solving requires a special kind of intelligence that he does not possess.

However, Bezos also acknowledges that **analysis should be used in decision-making when possible**. While working at D.E. Shaw & Co., Bezos told his boss, David Shaw, that he planned to leave to start Amazon. Shaw took Bezos on a walk and told him that starting Amazon would be a better idea for someone who didn’t already have a good job. Bezos thought about this advice for two days and ultimately decided to leave D.E. Shaw & Co. anyway.

 Bezos believes that **it is important to minimize regrets, and the biggest regrets in life come from things we didn’t try**. Bezos also believes that **thinking small can become a self-fulfilling prophecy**. Bezos thinks that people tend to overestimate risk and underestimate opportunity. He believes that entrepreneurs should try to be aware of this bias and adjust their thinking accordingly. **Essentially, you should go with your gut but be prepared to back it up with data when needed.**

**Bezos values data but recognizes its limitations.** He believes that **anecdotes are usually right when they disagree with data**, which indicates that the wrong things are being measured. He illustrated this point with a story about how he made a customer service call to demonstrate the long wait times to his team. He also warns against becoming too reliant on metrics or proxies, especially over long periods. Over time, people can forget why a certain metric was chosen in the first place, and the rationale behind it might become outdated. **The most junior person in a company should be able to overrule the most senior person if they have the data to support their point**.

**Overall, Bezos encourages a balance of intuition and data-driven decision-making, while being aware of the potential pitfalls of both approaches.** He describes himself as a “Wanderer” who follows his curiosity but works hard to achieve his goals.
