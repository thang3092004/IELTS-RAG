Jeff Bezos uses the concept of "one-way doors" and "two-way doors" to categorize decisions based on their reversibility and impact. This framework helps streamline decision-making and encourages appropriate levels of scrutiny for different situations. Here's how it works:

*   **Two-way door decisions are reversible and relatively low risk**. These decisions can be made quickly, often by individuals or small teams deep within the organization. If a two-way door decision proves to be wrong, it can be easily reversed without significant consequences. Bezos emphasizes that **most decisions fall into this category** and should be made with speed and agility. An example of a two-way door decision might be experimenting with a new marketing campaign or launching a new product feature that can be easily adjusted or rolled back if it doesn't perform as expected.
*   **One-way door decisions are irreversible or very difficult and costly to change**. They carry significant weight and require careful deliberation. These decisions should be elevated to senior executives who can slow down the process, gather more information, and ensure the right decision is made from the start. Examples of one-way door decisions might include major acquisitions, like Amazon's purchase of Whole Foods, or significant infrastructure investments, such as building a new fulfillment center. These decisions have long-term implications and are difficult to undo once implemented.

Bezos emphasizes that **it's crucial to distinguish between these two types of decisions** to avoid unnecessary delays and ensure that critical choices receive appropriate attention. He acknowledges that **companies often fall into the trap of using a "one size fits all" decision-making process, applying a heavy-handed approach even to reversible, low-impact decisions**. This can stifle innovation and slow down progress. 

By embracing the concept of one-way and two-way doors, Bezos promotes a culture of **decisiveness and calculated risk-taking**. He encourages teams to **move quickly on reversible decisions** while remaining **thoughtful and deliberate when facing irreversible choices**.
