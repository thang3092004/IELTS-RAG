At both Amazon and Blue Origin, Jeff Bezos has sought to establish a corporate culture that prioritizes **"truth-telling,"** which he describes as **"one of the things that happens in business, probably anything that you’re...where you have an ongoing program and something is underway for a number of years."**  

Bezos emphasizes that **"any high performing organization, whether it’s a sports team, a business...an activist group, I don’t care what it is, any high performing organization has to have mechanisms and a culture that supports truth-telling."** Bezos believes that **“we humans are not really truth-seeking animals. We are social animals.”**  Therefore, people will often avoid speaking hard truths in order to maintain social cohesion. This tendency is especially prevalent in the corporate world, where employees are often reluctant to voice dissenting opinions for fear of jeopardizing their careers.   

However, Bezos argues, **“important truths can be uncomfortable, they can be awkward, they can be exhausting.”** **“Truths are often, they don’t want to be heard.”**  Nevertheless, he stresses that companies need to implement strategies that promote truth-telling. He suggests that business leaders should:

*  **Start by acknowledging that truth-telling is not easy and requires a conscious effort:** Bezos says that **“[y]ou have to talk about that. And you have to talk about the fact that it takes energy to do that. And you have to talk to people, you have to remind people it’s okay that it’s uncomfortable. You have to literally tell people it’s not what we’re designed to do as humans.”** 
*  **Empower junior employees to challenge senior leadership if they have data to back up their claims:** Bezos explains that **“[y]ou want to set up your culture so that the most junior person can overrule the most senior person if they have data. And that really is about trying to, you know, there are little things you can do. So for example, in every meeting that I attend, the most junior person in the room goes first. And that’s so that they’re not influenced, or they have the opportunity to speak their mind in a kind of unfiltered way.”**  
*  **Encourage the expression of strong opinions as long as they are rooted in data:**  Bezos notes that  **“[s]ometimes it can even, by the way, a lot of our most powerful truths turn out to be hunches. They turn out to be based on anecdotes. They’re intuition based. And sometimes you don’t even have strong data...and that can be right. So intuition, gut, feeling, pattern recognition, those are real things too.”** 

Bezos’s emphasis on truth-telling likely stems from his belief in the importance of **“customer obsession”** — that is, a focus on understanding and meeting customer needs and desires. Bezos notes that **“[t]his is another really good and kind of deep question because there are big things that are really important to manage. And then there are small things [that are also important].”**   He offers the example of 1-click shopping, which he believes solved **“a number of paper cuts.”**  He explains that  **“[h]aving enough money to buy a thing and then buying it is a pleasant experience. And like having pain around that is somehow just you’re ruining a beautiful experience.”** 

In sum, Bezos’s concept of “truth-telling” is central to his business philosophy.  He believes that organizations need to foster a culture in which employees at all levels feel empowered to voice uncomfortable truths. In doing so, companies can effectively identify areas for improvement and continue to enhance the customer experience.
