## Understanding AHP and ANP Models in Super Decisions

The transition from an Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) model to an Analytic Network Process (ANP) model within the Super Decisions software is a structured process designed to accommodate more complex decision-making scenarios. While AHP is effective for hierarchical organizational structures, ANP offers flexibility by accounting for interdependencies among criteria and alternatives. This makes the conversion vital for assessments requiring an integrated approach.

### Steps for Converting AHP to ANP

1. **Access Sample Models**: Start the process by opening the sample models available within Super Decisions. This can be done by navigating through the Help menu and selecting the appropriate AHP model that serves as a foundation for your analysis.

2. **Remove Goal Node**: Unlike AHP, ANP does not utilize a goal cluster. Therefore, during the transition, it is essential to remove the goal node. This step simplifies the structure by allowing multiple clusters without defining a singular overarching goal.

3. **Establish Clusters**: In an ANP model, you can create distinct clusters for both criteria and alternatives. This allows the model to feature various criteria covered by different clusters, adapting to the need for flexibility in comparisons between criteria across various alternatives.

4. **Connecting Criteria and Alternatives**: One of the critical differences in ANP is the need to establish connections between the criteria and alternatives. Each criterion must be compared with respect to each alternative, which extends the decision-making structure beyond simple hierarchy to a web of interdependencies.

### Additional Judgments Required

Transitioning to an ANP model necessitates additional judgments that might not have been required in the AHP setting:

1. **Importance of Criteria**: Users must evaluate how important each criterion is in the context of every alternative. This involves making direct comparisons to ascertain the significance of one criterion relative to another.

2. **Interdependencies Among Alternatives**: ANP allows for considering how alternatives influence each other. This means adding judgments related to how changes in one alternative's performance may affect others, capturing the dynamics of complex decision scenarios.

3. **Prioritization by Relative Assessments**: Similar to AHP, users should still prioritize alternatives but with a focus on the relational assessments that consider all criteria simultaneously, enabling a more holistic view of the decision landscape.

### Conclusion

The conversion from AHP to ANP in Super Decisions enhances the model's capability to capture intricate relationships among criteria and alternatives. By systematically removing the goal node, creating multiple clusters, and establishing necessary connections, users can leverage the power of ANP. Additional judgments related to the importance of criteria and the interdependencies among alternatives are crucial to fully utilize the expanded analytical framework provided by ANP. This integrated approach facilitates informed decision-making, particularly for complex scenarios where traditional AHP may fall short.
