Creating an Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) hierarchy in SuperDecisions software involves several structured steps that facilitate decision-making by organizing complex criteria and alternatives. Here’s a comprehensive overview of the process:

### 1. **Defining the Goal**
The first step in establishing an AHP model is to define the primary goal or objective you aim to achieve through the decision-making process. In SuperDecisions, this goal is designated as the **Goal Node**. It serves as the top-level element of your hierarchy and focuses the entire analysis on reaching a specific decision or outcome. For instance, if you are selecting the best car model, the goal node will represent "Select the Best Car."

### 2. **Identifying Criteria**
Once the goal is defined, the next phase involves identifying the specific criteria that will be used to evaluate the alternatives. These criteria represent the factors that will influence your decision. In SuperDecisions, criteria can include aspects such as price, fuel efficiency, comfort, and prestige. Each criterion is added as a **Criteria Node** connected to the goal node, forming a structural framework. Organizing criteria into a **Criteria Cluster** helps manage and visualize the components relevant to your evaluation.

### 3. **Determining Alternatives**
After establishing the criteria, you’ll need to determine the alternatives you want to evaluate. These are the options that will be assessed against the criteria. In SuperDecisions, you will create an **Alternatives Cluster** where each alternative, such as specific car models (e.g., Acura TL, Honda Civic, Toyota Camry), is defined as a separate node connected to the respective criteria. This structuring allows users to visually compare how each alternative meets the specified criteria.

### 4. **Establishing Relationships**
With the nodes for the goal, criteria, and alternatives in place, the next step involves establishing relationships among them. You will create directed connections between the goal node, criteria nodes, and alternatives nodes within the SuperDecisions interface. These connections indicate the influence of each criterion on the overall goal.

### 5. **Making Pairwise Comparisons**
One of the core components of the AHP methodology is to perform pairwise comparisons to assess the relative importance of each criterion and the performance of the alternatives concerning each criterion. Users will input judgments that signify how much more one criterion or alternative is preferred over another. This is done using the **pairwise comparison matrix** feature within the software, which captures subjective judgments quantitatively.

### 6. **Calculating Priorities**
After pairwise comparisons are made, SuperDecisions processes the inputted judgments to calculate priorities across the connected nodes. The software will generate priority scores for alternatives within the context of the criteria, as well as weightings for the criteria themselves, reflecting their importance related to the goal.

### 7. **Synthesizing Results**
Finally, SuperDecisions synthesizes the results to present a comprehensive view of how each alternative ranks according to the defined criteria and goal. The output will include rankings and prioritizations that allow users to make informed decisions based on systematic evaluations.

### Conclusion
The AHP hierarchy within SuperDecisions software is a robust decision-making framework that helps users navigate through complex evaluations by providing clear structures, relationships, and data visualizations. By defining goals, establishing criteria and alternatives, and systematically assessing them through pairwise comparisons, users can confidently arrive at well-informed decisions that are grounded in structured analysis. This methodology highlights the software’s capabilities in facilitating comprehensive decision analysis across various applications.
