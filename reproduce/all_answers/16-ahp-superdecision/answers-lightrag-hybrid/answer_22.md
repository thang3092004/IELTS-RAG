## Synthesis of Models in SuperDecisions: With vs. Without Sub-Criteria

When using SuperDecisions for decision analysis, the process of synthesizing models can vary significantly depending on whether sub-criteria are involved. Understanding these differences can enhance a user's ability to evaluate alternatives effectively and make informed decisions.

### Synthesizing a Model Without Sub-Criteria

In a model without sub-criteria, the synthesis process is relatively straightforward. Users compare alternatives based on primary criteria directly associated with each alternative. For example, if you are evaluating car models based on the criteria of price, MPG, and comfort, the synthesis involves aggregating these criteria to determine overall preferences among the models. During this process, the user inputs judgments on how each alternative performs against each criterion. The analysis outputs results to indicate which alternative is preferred based on the aggregated assessments.

The synthesis may utilize basic methods such as the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP), where alternatives are ranked based on aggregated scores derived from direct comparisons. Since only primary criteria are considered, the interpretation of results is simplified, but it may not capture the nuanced decision factors that can be captured when sub-criteria are introduced.

### Synthesizing a Model With Sub-Criteria

On the other hand, synthesizing a model that incorporates sub-criteria introduces additional complexity. Each primary criterion can be broken down into multiple sub-criteria, allowing for a more detailed evaluation of alternatives. For instance, in the case of car evaluation, comfort might include sub-criteria such as seating capacity, interior features, and ride quality. This tiered approach offers a richer, more granular assessment of alternatives.

In this framework, users first evaluate sub-criteria and assign weights or scores to each sub-criterion relative to its contribution to the overarching primary criterion. After establishing these evaluations, the synthesis process aggregates results not only at the primary level but also at the sub-criteria level. This hierarchical approach often utilizes more advanced methods such as the Analytic Network Process (ANP), which accounts for dependencies and interrelationships between criteria.

The synthesis outputs in a model with sub-criteria provide a multi-dimensional view of alternatives. Consequently, users can identify the strengths and weaknesses of each alternative across multiple dimensions of assessment. This can be particularly beneficial when making decisions in complex scenarios, where multiple factors influence user preferences.

### Conclusion

In summary, the difference in synthesizing a model in SuperDecisions with and without sub-criteria lies primarily in the complexity and richness of the analysis. While models without sub-criteria offer a simplified comparison based solely on primary criteria, models with sub-criteria allow for a layered evaluation that captures more detailed insights into the alternatives being assessed. This layered approach can significantly enhance decision-making processes, especially in environments characterized by multifaceted evaluations and interdependencies among criteria.
