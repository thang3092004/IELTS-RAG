The absolute measurement process in AHP is used when there are many alternatives, making the relative measurement process impractical. It is implemented in two phases: the first phase involves building the model using the relative measurement process, and the second phase employs ratings.

**Ratings** are used to prioritize criteria that are less important. The number of ratings used for a criterion depends on its importance: fewer ratings are used for less important criteria. For example, a university might use six ratings for "Admission Test Result" and "CGPA," but only four for "Working Experience," "Letter of Recommendation," and "Extracurricular Activities."

**Intensities** are the individual ratings within a rating scale for a specific criterion. For instance, a rating scale for “Admission Test Result” could have intensities such as “Outstanding,” “Excellent,” “Very good,” “Good,” “Average,” and “Poor.” Each intensity is then compared pairwise to establish its priority within the rating scale.  For example, "Outstanding" might be judged as being strongly more important than "Poor."

Once the ratings and intensities for each criterion are defined, the performance of each alternative (e.g., a candidate for admission) is evaluated against the established rating scales. The software then calculates the weighted scores for each alternative based on their performance ratings and the priorities of the criteria and their intensities. This process results in a ranking of alternatives, allowing the decision-maker to select the best option based on the absolute measurement process.
