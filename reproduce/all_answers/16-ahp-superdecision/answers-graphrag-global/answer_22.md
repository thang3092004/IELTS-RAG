## Synthesizing Models with Sub-Criteria vs. Without Sub-Criteria in SuperDecisions

The process of synthesizing a model in SuperDecisions can greatly vary based on whether sub-criteria are incorporated or not. This distinction is vital as it influences the granularity, complexity, and overall effectiveness of the decision-making process.

### Models with Sub-Criteria

When synthesizing a model with sub-criteria, the evaluation process is more structured and detailed. Analysts break down main criteria into specific, hierarchical sub-criteria, facilitating a nuanced assessment of alternatives. This level of granularity allows decision-makers to prioritize and weigh the significance of various aspects within each main criterion systematically. For instance, if evaluating 'Health Impacts,' the sub-criteria might include 'Short-Term Health Risk' and 'Long-Term Health Consequences.' 

Such a comprehensive framework enables users to assess alternatives against well-defined benchmarks, which may lead to more informed and reliable outcomes. With the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) functionalities available in SuperDecisions, the inclusion of sub-criteria enhances transparency and rationality behind decisions, as each sub-criterion can be weighted according to its relevance. Moreover, this approach provides users with increased flexibility and customization in the evaluation process, allowing them to capture the interdependencies and relationships between criteria effectively.

### Models without Sub-Criteria

In contrast, synthesizing a model without sub-criteria results in a more generalized evaluation where alternatives are assessed based solely on main criteria. This streamlined approach simplifies the decision-making process as it eliminates the need for intricate analysis of sub-level factors. However, this simplicity comes at the cost of depth; vital nuances may be overlooked, potentially leading to less informed decision-making.

Without the incorporation of sub-criteria, users may struggle to identify specific influences within broader criteria, which could result in less accurate evaluations. By treating criteria as standalone elements, the process may miss out on important relationships or interactions between different criteria, limiting the overall effectiveness of the analysis.

### Conclusion

To summarize, synthesizing models in SuperDecisions with sub-criteria allows for a more detailed and flexible evaluation, enabling decision-makers to factor in complex interdependencies and nuances. Conversely, models synthesized without sub-criteria provide a simpler but potentially less thorough framework, as they might overlook critical elements essential for more informed decisions. Ultimately, the choice between using sub-criteria or not shall depend on the complexity of the decision context and the level of detail required for effective analysis.
