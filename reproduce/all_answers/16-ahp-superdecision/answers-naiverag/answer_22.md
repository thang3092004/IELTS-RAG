## Understanding Synthesis in SuperDecisions

In SuperDecisions, the process of synthesizing a decision-making model can vary significantly depending on whether the model incorporates sub-criteria or operates solely with higher-level criteria. The distinction is crucial as it affects how alternatives are evaluated and how results are interpreted.

### Synthesis with Sub-Criteria

When a model includes sub-criteria, the synthesis process typically involves multiple layers of evaluation. Users begin by establishing connections between the primary criteria and their respective sub-criteria. For example, in a car selection model, a main criterion such as "Comfort" could include sub-criteria like "Driving Performance" and "Ride Quality." This hierarchical structure allows for a more granular analysis of each alternative against various dimensions of a given criterion.

1. **Establishing Connections**: The first step involves linking alternatives to both the main criteria and sub-criteria. This gives a detailed structure, allowing each alternative to be compared based on how well it meets each sub-criterion.
  
2. **Entering Judgments**: Users must input specific judgments for each alternative in relation to both the criteria and sub-criteria. This provides a comprehensive reference point during synthesis.

3. **Synthesis Process**: The synthesis process for a model with sub-criteria computes priorities for each criterion and then delves deeper to assess the sub-criteria. The final outcomes reflect the relative significance of each criterion and sub-criterion, thereby delivering more nuanced insights into the best alternative.

In this multi-level synthesis, the software allows more sophisticated judgments to be made because each sub-criterion can distinctly influence the priority of the overall alternatives.

### Synthesis without Sub-Criteria

Conversely, when synthesizing a model that lacks sub-criteria, the evaluation remains at a higher, more generalized level. The focus is solely on the main criteria tied directly to alternatives, without the added complexity of breaking down evaluations into sub-criteria.

1. **Direct Comparisons**: In such models, alternatives are compared only against the main criteria. This simplification can save time and reduce the workload associated with entering judgments, but it also means that the depth of analysis is sacrificed.

2. **Judgments Entry**: Users still need to enter judgments for each alternative based only on the criteria, leading to a straightforward priority calculation without any breakdown into sub-components.

3. **Synthesis Process**: The synthesis then provides overall priorities based on the established criteria alone. As a result, the conclusions drawn can be less informative than those from models with sub-criteria since they do not account for more specific attributes influencing the decision.

### Conclusion

In summary, synthesizing a model with sub-criteria in SuperDecisions involves a more complex and thorough examination of alternatives through multiple layers of criteria evaluation. This approach allows for a detailed understanding of how well each alternative performs across various dimensions related to the main criteria. In contrast, models without sub-criteria tend to provide a more simplistic overview, focusing only on the primary criteria, which can lead to less nuanced insights. Depending on the decision-making context, using sub-criteria may significantly improve the decision quality by recognizing important subtleties in the alternatives being compared.
