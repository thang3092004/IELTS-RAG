### Understanding Global Priorities in AHP

The Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) is a structured decision-making tool that helps prioritize alternatives by breaking down a complex problem into simpler components. Using Super Decisions software, you can effectively calculate the global priorities of alternatives through a series of well-defined steps. Here’s a detailed breakdown of the process:

### Step 1: Establishing the Hierarchical Structure

1. **Define the Goal:** Start by clearly identifying the primary objective of your decision-making process (e.g., selecting the best car).
2. **Identify Criteria:** Determine the criteria that will influence the decision. These might include factors such as price, comfort, fuel efficiency, and reputation.
3. **List Alternatives:** Identify the alternatives that you will compare, like different car models (e.g., Honda Civic, Acura TL, Toyota Camry).

### Step 2: Constructing the AHP Model in Super Decisions

1. **Setting Up Clusters:** Use the Super Decisions interface to create clusters for the goal, criteria, and alternatives. This is essential for organizing your model into a hierarchical structure.
   - Create a cluster for the Goal.
   - Add clusters for each criterion and each alternative.
  
2. **Pairwise Comparisons:** Within the Super Decisions software, conduct pairwise comparisons for each criterion against the alternatives. For instance, compare how much more important price is compared to comfort when making a car selection. This is often done using a scale where values represent how many times one element is preferred over another.

### Step 3: Inputting Judgments and Calculating Local Priorities

1. **Entering Judgments:** Input your pairwise comparison judgments directly into the software. For example, if you believe that car A is twice as good as car B regarding price, you would assign a value of 2 to reflect this preference.
2. **Calculation of Local Priorities:** Once all comparisons are entered, Super Decisions can synthesize these judgments to calculate local priorities for criteria and alternatives. Local priorities reflect the relative importance of alternatives for each criterion.

### Step 4: Synthesizing the Results for Global Priorities

1. **Synthesize Model:** After entering all comparisons and judgments, navigate to the "Compute" section in Super Decisions and select “Synthesize.” This action merges local priorities across all levels of your hierarchy, resulting in global priorities for your alternatives.
2. **Reviewing Outcomes:** The results will show the global priorities for each alternative, allowing you to determine which option best aligns with your overall goal. These priorities indicate the relative strengths of each alternative based on the criteria you defined earlier.

3. **Output Interpretation:** Super Decisions will provide a summary with graphical representations to make the data easy to interpret. You should look for the highest global priority score to identify your optimal choice.

### Example of Application

As a practical demonstration, suppose you are evaluating three cars (Honda Civic, Acura TL, Toyota Camry) based on the criteria of price, comfort, and fuel efficiency. You would input your preferences into the software, engage in pairwise comparisons for each car regarding these criteria, and then synthesize the results to get the global priorities. The car with the highest global priority would typically be recommended as the best choice based on the weightings of your inputs.

### Conclusion

In summary, the process of calculating global priorities using AHP in Super Decisions involves defining a clear hierarchy, conducting pairwise comparisons, entering judgments, and synthesizing results. This methodical approach helps in simplifying complex decisions, making it easier to fulfill preferences through quantified analyses. By accurately applying these steps, decision-makers can ensure that they are supporting their choices with robust data-driven insights.
