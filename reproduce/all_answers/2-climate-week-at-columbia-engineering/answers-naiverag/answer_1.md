The slowdown of the triple product in fusion research during the late 1990s and early 2000s can be attributed to several key factors rooted in the challenges associated with fusion technology development.

### Technical Challenges
One of the major reasons is the increasing complexity and scale of fusion experiments. The pursuit of higher performance in fusion devices necessitated constructing larger and more sophisticated apparatuses. As researchers aimed for more significant advancements in fusion performance metrics, including the triple product (which measures the efficiency of plasma confinement), they found that reaching the next performance milestones often required constructing ever-larger devices.

### Economic Considerations
The financial implications of building such large-scale experiments also played a critical role. For example, the ITER project in France, which was a significant international fusion research endeavor, had escalated in cost to estimates ranging from $30 to $60 billion. This substantial investment raised economic concerns and complicated funding continuance, which hindered the overall progress of fusion research.

### Maturity of Technology
Furthermore, by the late 1990s into the early 2000s, fusion research began to plateau as the technology matured. Many experiments hit performance ceilings, and it became increasingly apparent that incremental advancements could not be achieved without shifting to entirely new approaches or significantly increasing investment. The situation reflected a critical period where the drive to build bigger and more complex fusion devices was essential, yet the results did not keep pace with earlier years of rapid advancements.

### Shift in Focus
Lastly, there was a shift in focus within the scientific community, emphasizing not just technological and engineering advancements but also the need for the reliability and straightforward construction methods of fusion devices. This shift necessitated a reevaluation of strategies, which, while promising in the long term, contributed to the slowdown observed during that era.

In summary, the convergence of these technical, economic, and strategic challenges contributed to the stagnation of the triple product in fusion research in the late 1990s and early 2000s, prompting a period of reassessment and reevaluation within the field.
