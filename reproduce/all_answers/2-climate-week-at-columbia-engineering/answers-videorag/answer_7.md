Researchers at Columbia University are actively engaged in several initiatives aimed at closing data gaps in air pollution monitoring, particularly in underserved regions both globally and within the United States. Here are some of the key approaches they employ:

### Deployment of Low-Cost Sensors
One significant strategy involves the development and deployment of low-cost air quality sensors. These devices offer an accessible means to gather data on pollutants such as PM2.5. For example, researchers showcased the creation of custom-built air quality monitors costing approximately $50, which is substantially cheaper than traditional research-grade monitors that can exceed $20,000. This democratization of technology enables communities, particularly in resource-limited areas, to participate in air quality monitoring efforts and collect valuable data on local pollution levels.

### Capacity Building and Training
The researchers emphasize capacity building as an essential component of their work. This includes training local researchers and stakeholders to effectively use monitoring equipment and understand air quality data. Columbia has undertaken various initiatives, such as scholar exchanges and certificate programs, to enhance local expertise and facilitate better data collection practices. Training efforts extend to communities in both the Global South and within the USA, particularly in low-income neighborhoods where data collection has previously been lacking.

### Use-Inspired Research
Columbia University researchers engage in "use-inspired" research, which means they focus on practical applications of their findings to inform public policy. This involves investigating the sources and concentrations of air pollutants in various regions, including cities in Africa such as Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of Congo. By identifying pollution sources, researchers provide actionable insights that local governments can use to develop targeted air quality action plans.

### Collaborative Efforts
Collaboration is key to Columbia’s strategy. The university works with institutions like the Westervelt Aerosol Group and receives funding from organizations such as the National Science Foundation (NSF). These partnerships enhance the scale and impact of their projects, allowing them to effectively gather and analyze air quality data across multiple regions.

### Data Science Techniques
The application of advanced data science techniques is another critical aspect of their efforts. Researchers utilize statistical methods to refine the data collected from low-cost sensors, improving its accuracy. By comparing raw sensor data to reference monitors, they develop correction factors that bolster data quality, making it more reliable for regulatory and public health decisions.

### Global Focus
The scale of their initiatives reflects a commitment to addressing global challenges. Specific projects have been implemented in various countries across Africa and involve careful monitoring of PM2.5 levels, revealing alarming pollution levels that frequently exceed World Health Organization guidelines. The data gathered informs global health discussions and emphasizes the need for comprehensive air quality measures.

Through these efforts, Columbia University not only works to fill data gaps but also empowers communities to understand and respond to air quality challenges that directly impact public health and environmental justice.
