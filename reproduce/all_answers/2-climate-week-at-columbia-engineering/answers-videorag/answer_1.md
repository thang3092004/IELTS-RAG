The stopping of the acceleration of the triple product in fusion research during the late 1990s and early 2000s can be attributed to several intertwined factors primarily related to the challenges and complexities associated with fusion technology development.

### Technical Challenges

1. **Increasing Complexity of Experiments**: Fusion experiments grew increasingly complicated as the demand for more advanced and larger-scale experiments took precedence. The primary focus shifted toward building larger devices, which required extensive resources, both in terms of funding and materials. The larger the device, the higher the technical risks involved, which in turn complicated the achievement of desired outcomes.

2. **Need for Bigger Devices**: Achieving a successful fusion reaction as measured by the triple product (the product of the plasma density, temperature, and confinement time) necessitated the construction of larger and more sophisticated devices. This was evident in the shift towards projects like the ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor), which represents a monumental engineering challenge with high cost estimates ranging from tens to hundreds of billions of dollars. This approach diverged from the earlier methods that demonstrated promise in achieving fusion at smaller scales.

### Funding and Investment Issues

3. **Resource Allocation**: By the late 90s, significant resources had already been diverted towards building large tokamak-style devices, which limited funding for alternative fusion concepts that could have provided competitive innovative breakthroughs. The closing off of funding pathways for new and promising fusion technologies slowed down the overall development progress.

4. **Funding Uncertainty**: The U.S. government and funding institutions began reevaluating their commitment to fusion research, leading to uncertainties regarding long-term funding and support. This context curtailed the experimental exploration of many potentially viable fusion avenues.

### Shift in Research Direction

5. **Research Focus**: The focus during this period generally shifted from theoretical and exploratory research to large-scale engineering projects aimed at achieving practical fusion energy. This change in direction sometimes neglected the critical investigations into alternative concepts that might provide paths to practical fusion energy.

### Summary

In essence, the cessation of the rapid progress in the triple product for fusion research in the late 1990s and early 2000s reflects a convergence of heightened engineering challenges, significant funding demands for larger devices, and a strategic shift towards established methodologies that limited broader exploratory initiatives. As a result, fusion research faced a plateau in its progress, marking a transition phase that necessitated a reevaluation of its approaches and goals for future advancements.
