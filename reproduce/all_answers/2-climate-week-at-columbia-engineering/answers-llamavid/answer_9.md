Low-cost air quality sensors are often used in developing countries due to their affordability and simplicity. However, their accuracy can be compromised by environmental conditions and interference. Researchers are working to improve the accuracy of these sensors by calibrating them to provide more reliable data. They do this by comparing the readings from the sensors to known concentrations of pollutants, such as PM2.5 particles. This calibration can be done in the laboratory or in the field. In the field, sensors can be compared to reference monitors that measure PM2.5 concentrations. In the laboratory, sensors can be calibrated by exposing them to known concentrations of PM2.5 particles. Researchers can also develop correction factors that can be applied to the sensor readings to account for environmental conditions and other factors that may affect accuracy. These factors can include temperature, humidity, and wind speed. By calibrating sensors in this way, researchers can provide more accurate data that can be used to inform policy and decision-making around air quality and health.
