The sources describe a number of engineering challenges related to the development of fusion energy.

**Magnets:**

* The sources highlight the need to develop **high-temperature superconducting (HTS) magnets** that can withstand extreme currents without quenching (losing superconductivity). These magnets are critical to creating the strong magnetic fields required to confine plasma at the necessary temperatures and pressures for fusion.
* The development of these magnets has seen progress in recent years, but their large-scale manufacturing presents a significant engineering hurdle. Fusion power plants will require many of these magnets, and ensuring a reliable and cost-effective supply chain is essential.

**Materials:**

* Fusion reactions produce high-energy neutrons, which can damage the materials used in fusion reactors. **Developing materials that are resistant to neutron radiation** is crucial for building durable and reliable reactors.
* **Lithium is a key material for various components**, including diverters and blankets, and further research and development is needed to optimize its application in fusion reactors.

**Complexity and Scale:**

* Building fusion power plants requires overcoming **immense engineering complexities**. The sources mention the difficulties of constructing devices with intricate linkages and the challenges of recovering from accidents or breakdowns.
* The **sheer scale of the engineering projects** involved is another significant hurdle. Fusion power plants will be large and complex facilities, requiring specialized expertise and collaboration across various engineering disciplines.

**Workforce Development:**

* A key challenge is the **need for a skilled workforce** capable of designing, building, and operating fusion power plants.
* The industry needs to attract and train a new generation of engineers and scientists, including plasma physicists, cryogenic engineers, economists, operators, and experts in fields like material science and nuclear regulation.

**Other Challenges:**

* **Cryogenic engineering:** Maintaining the extremely low temperatures needed for superconductivity in magnets is a significant engineering challenge.
* **Plasma control:** Controlling the extremely hot and turbulent plasma within the reactor is a complex engineering and physics problem.
* **Tritium breeding:** Tritium, a rare and radioactive isotope of hydrogen, is a key fuel for fusion reactions. Developing efficient methods for breeding tritium within the reactor is crucial.
* **Integration with the grid:** Connecting fusion power plants to the existing electrical grid and ensuring their safe and reliable operation present engineering challenges.

Addressing these engineering challenges will require sustained research and development efforts, collaboration between industry, academia, and government, and significant investment.
