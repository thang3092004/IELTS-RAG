Researchers use a variety of techniques to calibrate low-cost air quality sensors and make them more useful for a variety of purposes.

To calibrate low-cost sensors, researchers compare data from low-cost sensors to data from regulatory-grade monitors. The regulatory monitors are more expensive but provide more accurate measurements. **By comparing the data from the two types of sensors, researchers can develop correction factors to apply to the data from the low-cost sensors.** These correction factors help to improve the accuracy of the low-cost sensors.

**One example of a project that used this technique was conducted in Accra, Ghana.** Researchers deployed a network of low-cost PM2.5 sensors throughout the city. They then compared the data from the low-cost sensors to data from regulatory-grade monitors. By applying correction factors, they were able to produce high-quality data from the low-cost sensors.

Researchers are also exploring using machine learning to improve the accuracy of low-cost sensors. **For example, researchers could train a machine learning model to identify and correct for biases in the data from low-cost sensors.** This approach could help to automate the calibration process and make it easier to deploy low-cost sensors in a variety of settings.

In addition to calibration, researchers are also working to develop new low-cost sensor technologies. **One promising approach is to use 3D printing to create sensors.** This approach could significantly reduce the cost of sensors and make them more accessible to communities around the world.

The use of low-cost sensors has the potential to revolutionize air quality monitoring. By making sensors more affordable and accessible, researchers can empower communities to take action to improve air quality.
