The reason why progress in fusion research, as measured by the triple product, slowed down in the late 1990s and early 2000s is because **scientists determined that to reach higher levels of performance, they would need to build larger and larger experimental devices**. This realization led to the construction of ITER, a massive international fusion project in southern France. ITER is a tokamak, which is the most common type of fusion device.

According to the sources, the cost of building ITER was estimated to be between $30 and $60 billion, and it would take decades to complete. This made it clear that continuing to scale up the size of fusion devices was not a sustainable approach.

Instead, fusion researchers and companies have begun to focus on other ways to improve fusion performance. This includes:

*   Developing **new magnet technologies, such as high-temperature superconducting magnets**, that allow for stronger magnetic fields and smaller devices.
*   **Exploring alternative fusion concepts**, such as spherical tokamaks and magnetic mirrors, that may offer advantages in terms of cost, complexity, or performance.
*   **Forming partnerships with private companies** to accelerate the development and commercialization of fusion energy.

These efforts have led to a renewed sense of optimism in the fusion community, with many experts now believing that fusion power could be a reality within the next few decades.
