Prompt caching and traditional Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) are both techniques used to enhance the efficiency of language models. 

Prompt caching involves storing the results of previous prompts so that if the same or similar prompt is encountered again, the cached result can be used instead of recomputing the answer. This can significantly reduce computational costs and improve response times, especially in scenarios where the same queries are frequently repeated.

Traditional RAG, on the other hand, involves retrieving relevant information from a large corpus of documents to augment the generation process. While RAG can provide more contextually relevant and up-to-date information, it typically requires more computational resources due to the need for real-time retrieval and processing of external data.

In terms of cost and efficiency, prompt caching is generally more cost-effective and efficient for repeated queries, as it avoids redundant computations. However, RAG is more versatile and can provide more accurate and contextually relevant responses for unique or complex queries that require external information.
