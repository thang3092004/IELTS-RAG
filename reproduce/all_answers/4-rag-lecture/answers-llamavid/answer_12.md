While RAG systems have many benefits, they also have some limitations. 

One major limitation is that they rely on a predefined chunking strategy. This means that the same chunks will be used for every query, which can be problematic for long documents. For example, if a user's question requires more context from a specific part of a document, the chunks may not contain enough information.

Another limitation is the lack of flexibility in the chunking strategy. The same chunks may not be optimal for all queries. The chunking strategy is hard-coded into the RAG system, making it difficult to adapt to different types of questions.

Additionally, the chunks are pre-generated and cannot be updated in real-time. If a user's question changes during a conversation, the chunks may not be able to be updated quickly enough to provide a relevant answer.

Finally, the chunks are static and do not change over time. If a document changes, the chunks will not be updated, resulting in outdated context.

In summary, while RAG systems are useful for answering simple questions, they have some limitations that make them unsuitable for more complex or dynamic questions.
