Small open-source LLMs with less than 13 billion parameters are more affordable and accessible than larger models, but they face limitations when used for multi-agent systems. 

Here are some of the challenges faced when using these models:

*   **Limited Cognition**: Smaller models have less computational power, leading to less advanced reasoning, comprehension, and problem-solving abilities. They struggle with complex multi-agent interactions and tasks that demand deeper understanding.
*   **Lack of Function Calling Fine-tuning**: Many small open-source models are not trained specifically for function calling, hindering their ability to use tools and interact with external systems effectively. This is crucial for multi-agent systems that rely on agents' ability to execute actions and access information.
*   **Subpar Performance with Skills**: Even when equipped with skills, small models often fail to use them effectively. In a test where agents were tasked to find the price of a product and then write a marketing headline with the price, the models struggled to consistently fetch the correct price and incorporate it into the headline. This highlights the difficulty in getting small LLMs to perform even seemingly straightforward tasks within a multi-agent system.
*   **Incompatibility Issues**: Smaller models can face compatibility issues with evolving software like Autogen and LM Studio, requiring frequent updates and troubleshooting. For example, the Cod llama model was reported to have compatibility issues with LM Studio after updates, making it unusable despite its potential for coding tasks.

Despite these challenges, smaller open-source LLMs can still be valuable for multi-agent systems, especially with proper fine-tuning and optimization. As the field progresses, new techniques and models will likely emerge, further enhancing the capabilities of these models.
